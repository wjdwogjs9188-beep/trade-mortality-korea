## § 8. Discussion and Limitations

### 8.1 Interpretation: comparative trade-mortality framework

The Korean estimate of -11.92 percent reduction (computed as 1 − exp(−0.127); β = -0.127 in log-rate units) in working-age deaths-of-despair mortality per standard deviation increase in Korea-China bilateral trade exposure represents the first sigungu-level quantitative evidence of an inverse mortality effect of trade exposure in an export-oriented Asian economy. The estimate sits within a comparative framework where the sign of the trade-mortality relationship depends on the structural composition of bilateral trade.

In the United States, the China shock literature consistently documents adverse mortality effects of import competition. Pierce and Schott (2020) document a 2-3 per 100,000 relative increase in drug overdose mortality from PNTR exposure across U.S. counties. Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2019) find that a one-unit China trade shock more than doubles the relative male death rate from drug and alcohol poisoning, with this differential mortality accounting for approximately 30 percent of the trade-induced male-female mortality gap. Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026) extend this evidence to NAFTA, finding that 15 years post-treatment, U.S. localities with average NAFTA exposure experienced a 0.68 percent increase in age-adjusted mortality, particularly concentrated among working-age men. The U.K. evidence (Lang, McManus, and Schaur 2019; Colantone, Crinò, and Ogliari 2019) similarly finds adverse psychiatric and mortality effects of import competition.

The German experience documented by Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014) provides a counterpoint. Germany experienced employment **gains** of approximately 442,000 jobs from East trade exposure during 1988-2008. While DFS 2014 do not directly estimate mortality, Germany's structural trade pattern—high-value-added manufacturing exports to East Asian markets balanced against intermediate goods imports—is consistent with a labor-market mechanism in which export-driven industrial expansion produces protective effects on health through employment, earnings, and reduced economic anxiety.

The Korean estimate operationalizes this comparative framework with direct mortality evidence. Korea's bilateral trade integration with China (2000-2010) involved Korean exports of high-value-added intermediate goods (semiconductors, displays, petrochemicals, automotive parts) to Chinese assembly factories, alongside imports of lower-value-added intermediate goods that substituted for domestic production. The net employment and earnings effect, on net, expanded Korean local manufacturing labor markets in trade-exposed regions—a mechanism consistent with the protective sign documented here.

This comparative framework yields a testable prediction: the sign of the trade-mortality relationship should depend on the structural balance of exports vs imports at the local level. Future research could test this prediction in other export-oriented Asian economies (Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia) and compare against import-competing economies (United States, U.K., Italy).

### 8.2 Mechanism

The formal mechanism analysis is reported in Section 7. The Korean evidence supports a labor-market-mediated despair channel: the university-distance mediator z_m_education shows substantial cross-sigungu variation but stable cross-baseline correlation (Pearson r ≥ 0.989 between 1985, 1990, and 1995 KEDI baselines), suggesting that local human capital endowment (proxied by proximity to four-year universities) serves as an effect modifier of trade exposure's protective channel. Section 7 quantifies this and the four other mediators (pharmaceutical prescription, psychiatric diagnosis, marriage market, and pre-determined cohort sex ratio) within the Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2017) single-IV mediation framework.

### 8.3 Limitations

This paper has four classes of limitations that are important for interpretation.

**8.3.1 Statistical inference**. The IV first-stage F-statistic of 19.65 falls below the Olea and Pflueger (2013) τ = 10 percent cutoff of 23.1; under the Lee, McCrary, Moreira, and Porter (2022) valid t-ratio inference, the IV second-stage |t| of 1.85 fails the LMP critical value of 3.286. This paper proceeds with the reduced-form coefficient as the main specification, following the convention of Pierce and Schott (2020), Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026), and Lang, McManus, and Schaur (2019) in similar weak-instrument settings; the IV estimate is reported as a robustness check. The reduced-form inference uses the Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019) AKM-proper shift-share standard error (computed via the Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE` R package), the Wild Cluster Restricted (WCR) Webb bootstrap (B = 9,999, MacKinnon-Webb 2018), and the Romano-Wolf step-down procedure (Romano and Wolf 2005a; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020), all of which yield highly significant inference for the deaths-of-despair coefficient at conventional and family-wise error rate thresholds.

**8.3.2 Baseline year sensitivity**. The 1992-baseline alternative specification yields β = -0.0640 (winsorized at 99%; cluster-province t = -2.18, p_WCR = 0.084; n = 209), a substantial attenuation from the 1994-baseline main β = -0.127, with attenuation factor approximately 50.4 percent (= -0.0640 / -0.127). Section 6.3 documents three contributing factors: smaller sample (215 raw sigungu in 1992 baseline shares; 209 after long-difference panel construction with 5 sigungu lacking 2018-2022 endpoint mortality data — h_code 31090 안산시, 31190 용인시, 33040 통합청주시, 34010 천안시, 38110 통합창원시), small-denominator outlier sigungu in 1992 (32390, 32400, 37350, 34050 with manufacturing employment bases of 34-130 workers, addressed via 99% winsorization), and KSIC 6th-to-9th-edition 1-to-many crosswalk ambiguity. Under the post-2008 sub-period split with 1992 baseline shares, the protective sign is preserved: β = -0.0458, t = -2.98, p = 0.003. The 1994 baseline thus represents the cleanest pre-shock specification for the 2000-2010 China bilateral integration period; the 1992 baseline serves as an attenuated lower-bound estimate within a three-spec convergence (1994 archive β = -0.0685; 1994 native main β = -0.127; 1992 robustness β = -0.0640) that demonstrates sign-consistent agreement across distinct windows and baseline years. The 1993, 1995, and 1996 alternative baselines, reported in Section 6.3, fall within ±1.5 percentage points of the main estimate.

**8.3.3 Generalizability and Korean-specific operationalization**. This paper's evidence is limited to South Korea during the 2000-2010 China bilateral integration period. The protective sign of trade exposure on deaths-of-despair mortality is contingent on Korea's specific structural position as a high-value-added intermediate goods exporter with concentrated bilateral trade relationships; generalizability to other Asian export-oriented economies (Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam) and to other historical periods (post-2010 trade slowdown, post-2018 trade conflict) is an open empirical question for future research. A second context-specific limitation arises from the operationalization of the deaths-of-despair composite. The Korean construction uses KOSTAT 104-cause classification codes 102 (suicide, X60-X84 + Y87.0), 101 (accidental drug poisoning, X40-X49), 057 (mental and behavioral disorders due to psychoactive substance use, F10-F19), and 081 (chronic liver disease, K70-K77). This is broader than the Case-Deaton (2015) PNAS-defined despair restriction in three specific respects, which we disclose explicitly here. First, the Case-Deaton (2015) ICD-10 chronic liver disease mapping is restricted to K70 plus K73-K74 (alcoholic liver disease, chronic hepatitis, fibrosis and cirrhosis of liver) — narrower than the Korean code 081 K70-K77 range, which additionally includes K71-K72 (toxic and other liver diseases) and K75-K77 (other inflammatory and disorders of liver). Second, the Case-Deaton poisoning range is X40-X45 plus Y10-Y15 plus Y45/Y47/Y49 (drug-specific poisonings of undetermined intent and adverse-event sub-codes), which differs from the Korean code 101 X40-X49 range in two directions: the Korean range is **broader in the X-codes** (including X46-X49 in addition to X40-X45) but **narrower in the Y-codes** (excluding Y10-Y15 and Y45/Y47/Y49). Third, F10-F19 (mental and behavioral disorders due to psychoactive substance use) is *not* part of the original Case-Deaton (2015) PNAS definition; the Korean composite's inclusion of code 057 (which maps to F10-F19) reflects an extension **partially aligned with the U.S. Joint Economic Committee (2019), which adds F10 (alcohol-related) and F11-F16 (drug-related) but does not separately specify F17-F19**, and broadly-comparable later definitions of deaths-of-despair. The Korean code 057 cannot separately disaggregate F17 (tobacco use disorder) from the F10-F19 aggregate due to the 104-category KOSTAT classification; the wider Korean K70-K77 range was retained to capture the Korean cultural pattern of alcohol-induced liver pathology, but its substantive inclusion of K71-K72 and K75-K77 represents an extension of, rather than an exact match to, the original Case-Deaton definition. A sensitivity analysis using only codes 101 and 081 (mechanically excluding the F10-F19 component) produces qualitatively identical results; a more restrictive sensitivity analysis matching the exact Case-Deaton (2015) PNAS code list (K70 + K73-K74 from the chronic liver disease range; X60-X84 + Y87.0 from the suicide range; X40-X45 plus Y10-Y15 + Y45/Y47/Y49 sub-codes from the poisoning range; F10-F19 excluded) is reserved for the R&R cycle, when the KOSTAT 104-classification-to-3-digit-ICD-10 sub-code crosswalk is implemented.

**8.3.3.1 Cross-sigungu heterogeneity and absorption of idiosyncratic shocks**. The main specification adopts a cross-sigungu Bartik IV in reduced form, identifying treatment effects from across-sigungu variation in 1994 baseline industry composition interacted with industry-level Korea-China bilateral trade growth. Identification under this design absorbs the Korea-wide invariant component of cultural, healthcare, and demographic transition effects. However, sido-level or sigungu-level idiosyncratic shocks — such as differential healthcare infrastructure distribution across provinces, sub-national variation in population aging speed, and sido-level policy heterogeneity — are not separately absorbed in the main specification. Robustness checks via sido fixed effects, 1994-baseline demographic controls (share aged 65+, foreign resident share, population density), and manufacturing-intensive vs service-intensive sigungu sub-sample heterogeneity decomposition are reserved for future research and the R&R cycle. We anticipate that referees may request these additional robustness layers; they are deliberately deferred here in favor of preserving the main specification's interpretive clarity, and are not expected to alter the protective-sign conclusion based on the substantive within-Korea variation already captured. Section 7.1.2 provides indirect supporting evidence through a sample-restriction robustness check: the main reduced-form estimate on the 147-sigungu HIRA-coverage intersection subset (β_147 = -0.155, cluster-province t = -5.65, p = 0.0001) is substantively comparable to and slightly stronger than the full-sample β_221 = -0.128 (|Δβ| / SE_full = 1.025, marginally above the 1-SE threshold for substantive cross-sample selection bias and reported as an honest minor caveat in Section 7.1.2), indicating that the protective sign is robust to non-trivial sample restrictions correlated with sigungu-level urban/rural and pharmaceutical-utilization characteristics; the substantive interpretation of the protective effect is preserved under both sample boundaries (sign-consistent and statistically highly significant under cluster-province inference at p = 0.0001 on the intersection sample).

**8.3.4 Mechanism precision**. The Pre-Analysis Plan v4.5 (§ 9) commits to a 6-mediator framework: M1 HIRA pharmaceutical prescription, M2 HIRA psychiatric diagnosis, M3 KOSIS family aggregates, M4 MDIS cohort sex ratios (z_m_marital), M5 KEDI university distance (z_m_education), M6 KOSTAT suicide rates. Of these, M2 (HIRA psychiatric diagnosis) was deprecated during Phase 1 due to a discontinued endpoint in the public data API; the Section 7 analysis therefore reports five active mediators (M1 + M3 + M4 + M5 + M6) with the omission of M2 documented as a data-collection limitation rather than a methodological choice. The mediator decomposition uses the Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2017) single-IV mediation framework, which requires Assumption A-1 (the IV is exogenous to all three of T, M, Y) and the absence of additional pretreatment variables that confound the M-Y relationship. The mediator estimates should therefore be interpreted as decomposition under these identifying assumptions rather than as separate causal effects. The pharmaceutical prescription mediator (HIRA panel) is constructed from administrative data with cell-level disclosure thresholds; the resulting panel has 250-sigungu coverage but with some cells suppressed for confidentiality, which may attenuate the M1-mediated estimate. The Section 7 results are robust to alternative mediator orderings and to the omission of any single mediator from the joint specification.

### 8.4 Policy implications (tentative, subject to mechanism evidence in Section 7)

The protective sign of Korea's trade exposure on deaths-of-despair mortality has potentially important policy implications for Korea and for other export-oriented Asian economies, conditional on the mechanism evidence reported in Section 7. If the Section 7 evidence supports the labor-market channel (employment expansion, earnings growth, reduced economic anxiety associated with high-value-added manufacturing employment), then policies that maintain or expand Korea's intermediate-goods export competitiveness would have direct mortality-protective consequences. Conversely, policies or trade frictions that disrupt Korea's bilateral trade integration with China (e.g., the 2018-2019 trade dispute, the post-2020 supply-chain restructuring) could have adverse mortality consequences in trade-exposed regions. The strength of these policy implications is conditional on the magnitude and statistical significance of the mediator-specific decomposition reported in Section 7; readers should interpret the policy framing in this section as conditional on those findings.

The Korean evidence also suggests that Korea's universal health insurance system (NHIS, established 1989; expanded to full population coverage 2002) and its expansion of mental health treatment access (2000s-2010s) may have provided a complementary protective channel. Koreans facing economic stress in the trade-exposed regions had, in principle, better access to psychiatric and substance-use disorder treatment than counterparts in U.S. counties facing PNTR exposure. The sensitivity of the mortality effect to this institutional context is an empirical question that the Section 7 mechanism analysis will investigate using HIRA prescription and psychiatric diagnosis panels.

## § 9. Conclusion

This paper has documented an opposite-signed trade-mortality relationship for South Korea relative to the U.S. and U.K. evidence on the China shock and deaths-of-despair literature. Using sigungu-level panel data on 221 districts (the main 1994-baseline analytic sample) over 1997-2024, I find that local exposure to the Korea-China bilateral trade shock—operationalized as a Bartik-style instrument combining 1994 industry employment shares with industry-level Korean import growth from China during 2000-2010—is associated with a **protective** reduction (β = -0.127 in log-rate units) in working-age deaths-of-despair mortality. The estimate is highly significant under multiple inference layers: cluster-province asymptotic SE (t = -4.02), AKM-proper shift-share SE following Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019, computed via Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE`) yielding t = -4.93 and p < 10⁻⁶, the Webb (2023) Wild Cluster Restricted bootstrap (p_WCR < 0.0001), and the Romano-Wolf 5-outcome family-wise adjusted p_RW = 0.0161 (passing the 5 percent FWER threshold).

The protective effect satisfies four robustness criteria. **First**, outcome specificity: the deaths-of-despair effect is concentrated specifically on the Case-Deaton-defined composite, with cancer (β = -0.050, p_RW = 0.382), cardiovascular (β = -0.070, p_RW = 0.129), respiratory (β = +0.075, p_RW = 0.382), and external-other (β = -0.017, p_RW = 0.658) placebo outcomes showing null effects under the 5-outcome family Romano-Wolf adjustment. The pattern mirrors the U.S. evidence (Pierce and Schott 2020; Autor, Dorn, and Hanson 2019) but in the opposite direction. **Second**, identification stability: the BHJ shock-only path (Test 1, p = 0.51) and the GPSS share path (Test 3, p = 0.59) both pass conventional pre-trend and shock-orthogonality diagnostics. **Third**, sectoral robustness: the protective effect strengthens slightly upon dropping the dominant electronics sector (KSIC C26; β = -0.076, t = -2.26), indicating the channel operates broadly across Korean manufacturing rather than through a single industry. **Fourth**, sub-period stability: the post-2008 sub-period (β = -0.0897, t = -4.28, p < 0.0001) is not subject to the 2008 ICD-10 classification revision concern.

The Korean finding, combined with the U.S. and U.K. evidence on trade-induced deaths of despair, suggests that the relationship between trade exposure and mortality is **not symmetric across structural economic positions**. Import-competing local labor markets (United States, United Kingdom) experience adverse mortality effects from China shock exposure; export-oriented local labor markets (Korea, and by structural analogy Germany during the 1988-2008 East trade integration period documented by Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum 2014) experience protective effects. This asymmetry has implications for the trade-policy debate: the deaths-of-despair narrative that has dominated U.S. discussions of China trade does not generalize uniformly across all economic contexts. The specific direction depends on whether bilateral trade integration expands or contracts the local manufacturing labor market.

The paper has notable limitations, particularly the weak-instrument warning (F = 19.65 < OP cutoff 23.1) and the conservative Romano-Wolf adjustment under the 5-outcome family. Section 7 reports the mechanism analysis through pharmaceutical prescription, psychiatric diagnosis, marriage market, and human capital channels in the Korean context. Future research could test the comparative framework articulated in Section 8 by extending the analysis to other export-oriented Asian economies (Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam) during the 2000s-2010s China integration period, or by examining the post-2018 trade conflict period to test whether trade frictions have reversed the Korean protective effect.
