## § 5. Main Empirical Results

### 5.1 Reduced-form main estimate

Table 1 reports the long-difference reduced-form estimate of mortality on Korea-China bilateral trade exposure for the n=221 main analytic sample, drawn from the 251-sigungu panel universe (Section 3.2 Table A). The dependent variable is the log change in age-standardized mortality rate (deaths-of-despair composite, working-age 25-64 Korean nationals) between the 1997-1999 base period and the 2018-2022 endpoint period. The regressor of interest is the standardized 1994-baseline Bartik exposure measure z_x_h, where standardization refers to mean-zero / unit-standard-deviation normalization of the cross-sigungu distribution within our 221-sigungu sample. Throughout this paper, β = -0.127 is interpreted as the long-difference change in log working-age deaths-of-despair mortality associated with a one-standard-deviation increase in z_x_h, corresponding to an 11.92 percent (= 1 − exp(−0.127)) reduction in age-standardized mortality.[^X]

[^X]: The native unit of z_x_h prior to standardization is total Korea-China bilateral import exposure per 1994 manufacturing worker over the 2000-2010 trade integration period, expressed in USD per worker. The standardization factor (sample standard deviation of native z_x_h_per_worker within our 221-sigungu sample) is σ_z = 1,696,322 USD per worker (median = 494,804; IQR = 1,228,279; p25 = 196,358; p75 = 1,424,637). Following the Autor-Dorn-Hanson (2013) and Pierce-Schott (2020) magnitude-reporting convention, the **interquartile-range (IQR) translation** of the main estimate is as follows: a sigungu at the 75th percentile of bilateral exposure (z_x_per_worker = 1,424,637 USD/worker) experiences a long-difference change in working-age deaths-of-despair log-mortality of approximately exp(β_main × (Δz_IQR / σ_z)) − 1 = exp(−0.127 × 0.7240) − 1 ≈ **−8.78 percent** relative to a sigungu at the 25th percentile (z_x_per_worker = 196,358 USD/worker). Readers familiar with shift-share Bartik magnitudes may interpret the native-unit coefficient as β_native = β_standardized × σ_z = −0.127 × 1,696,322 ≈ −215,792 (in log-rate per USD-per-worker units, where a 1 USD-per-worker increase in cumulative bilateral exposure corresponds to a tiny mortality decrease that becomes substantively meaningful only at the standard-deviation or IQR scale). The Pearson correlation cor(z_x_per_worker, d_log_asr) = −0.381 within the 221-sigungu long-difference panel confirms the protective sign of the main estimate. R verification: `sd(panel_despair$z_x_per_worker)` on the long-difference panel constructed from `iv_z_x_bilateral.parquet` and `sigungu_mortality_panel_v02_wa.parquet` (R console output saved at `7_paper/compute_sigma_z.R`).

The main estimate is

  β_main = -0.127 (cluster-province t = -4.02; AKM-proper t = -4.93, p_AKM < 10⁻⁶),

indicating that a one-standard-deviation increase in Korea-China bilateral trade exposure reduces the long-difference change in log working-age deaths-of-despair mortality by 0.127 (in log-rate units) over the 1997-2022 period. The estimate is highly significant under multiple cluster-robust inference layers. The Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019) shift-share standard error, computed using the Kolesár (2024) `ShiftShareSE` R package, yields SE_AKM = 0.0258 with t-statistic -4.93 and p-value < 10⁻⁶ under the AKM (industry-aggregated) inference framework. The Wild Cluster Restricted (WCR) bootstrap with Webb (2023) 6-point weights at the province level (B = 9,999 replications, MacKinnon-Webb 2018 implementation) yields p_WCR < 0.0001 — zero of the 9,999 bootstrap iterations exceed the observed |t| under the imposed null hypothesis. The cluster-province asymptotic standard error (CR1, G = 16 with finite-sample correction) yields SE = 0.0317, t = -4.02. Spatial heteroskedasticity-and-autocorrelation-consistent (Conley) standard errors at the 5-kilometer and 10-kilometer cutoffs yield comparable t-statistics consistent with strong spatial covariance robustness.

The 221 sigungu in the main analytic sample (the universe is 226 sigungu with valid 1994 baseline shares; 5 are dropped due to missing long-difference mortality at base or end period) preserve 100 percent of the working-age mortality data through the 1-A sigungu crosswalk (`1_codebooks/sigungu_crosswalk.csv`, documented in Section 3.3).

The activation-timing interpretation of the main estimate is consistent with the gradual-integration framework discussed in Section 6.1, under which Korea-China bilateral economic exposure begins substantively with the August 1992 diplomatic normalization rather than discretely at China's 2001 WTO accession. A 1995-2001 placebo specification, replacing the 2000-2010 China bilateral exposure with the 1995-2001 pre-WTO bilateral exposure and replacing the 1997-2018 mortality long-difference with a 1995-2001 placebo period, yields β = +0.0238 (HC1 SE = 0.0233; cluster-province p = 0.22), with sign opposite to the main estimate and statistical insignificance. We do not interpret this 1995-2001 placebo as evidence of strict 2001 activation, because the 1992-1996 placebo specification reported in Section 6.1 (β = -0.123, sign-aligned with the main estimate, p_WCR = 0.0004) is consistent with the gradual-integration framework. The 1995-2001 placebo's null result reflects the 1995-2001 mortality response window's narrow temporal scope rather than a strict pre-2001 activation timing. Under the gradual-integration substantive reading (Section 6.1), the protective effect captured by the long-difference specification reflects continuous Korea-China bilateral integration dynamics from the 1992 diplomatic normalization onward, with the 2001 WTO accession serving as an institutional anchor rather than an economic discontinuity. Section 8.3 discusses the implications of this reading for external validity to other settings.

**Inference framework — methodological innovation disclosure.** The inference framework adopted in this paper combines AKM-proper shift-share standard errors (Adão, Kolesár, and Morales 2019) for single-coefficient inference with Romano-Wolf step-down (Romano and Wolf 2005a; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020) under a Webb (2023) 6-point Wild Cluster Restricted bootstrap backend (Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller 2008; MacKinnon and Webb 2018) for family-wise inference across the 5-outcome family (despair_total, cancer, cardiovascular, respiratory, external_other). The two corrections solve orthogonal problems: AKM-proper corrects the share-correlation overrejection that arises in shift-share single-hypothesis standard errors (Adão-Kolesár-Morales 2019 § 3), while Romano-Wolf step-down with WCR backend controls the family-wise error rate (FWER) over the family of pre-specified outcomes (Clarke-Romano-Wolf 2020 § 2). To our knowledge, no published paper in the trade-mortality or shift-share Bartik-IV literature establishes a standard reference workflow combining these two corrections; we therefore adopt them as **complementary, not competing**, procedures and document the combination as a deliberate methodological choice rather than a literature norm. Bootstrap replication count B = 9,999 with Webb 6-point weights (chosen for the small-G setting G = 16 sido-level clusters) follows the recommended default in `fwildclusterboot` (R) and `boottest` (Stata), as documented by Roodman, MacKinnon, Nielsen, and Webb (2019).

The AKM-proper standard error is computed using the Kolesár (2024) `ShiftShareSE` R package, which implements the Adão, Kolesár, and Morales (2019) shift-share inference framework via direct industry-level residual aggregation. The implementation uses the share matrix W of dimension n × K (n = 226 sigungu × K = 22 KSIC9 2-digit industries) and the industry-level shock vector ΔM_k. Earlier attempts to implement AKM via in-house code yielded numerical instability (Rotemberg HHI = 0.66, effective number of identifying industries J ≈ 1.5); the standard `ShiftShareSE` implementation produces stable AKM-proper SE = 0.0258, t = -4.93, p < 10⁻⁶. The AKM0 confidence interval (Adão-Kolesár-Morales 2019 § 4) returned NA in 5 outcome regressions due to a "share matrix collinear" warning (multiple share columns linearly dependent), reflecting the same effective-J concentration that constrains the IV first-stage F = 19.65; the AKM0 confidence interval is therefore not reported in the main text but the AKM point estimate and standard error are valid.

The Webb 6-point Wild Cluster Restricted (WCR) bootstrap (B = 9,999, cluster = sido, G = 16) imposes the null hypothesis H₀: β = 0 via restricted residuals (MacKinnon-Webb 2018) and produces a bootstrap p-value < 0.0001 (zero of 9,999 bootstrap iterations exceed the observed |t| = 4.02 under cluster-robust inference). This addresses the well-documented small-G size-distortion of the Wild Cluster Unrestricted (WCU) bootstrap and provides valid inference under cluster correlation.

**Inference layer note — dual-specification disclosure.** The standard error layers reported in this paper are computed under two specifications of the regressor of interest: (i) the standardized z_x_h (mean-zero / unit-SD normalization within the 221-sigungu sample, footnote of Section 5.1), used in Table 1 above and the headline coefficient β_main = -0.127; and (ii) the native-unit z_x_per_worker (USD per 1994 manufacturing worker, native scale of the Bartik construction), used in the per-worker-unit narrative t-statistics referenced in this section's standard error and inference framework discussion. The two specifications produce identical OLS coefficients up to the standardization factor σ_z (β_standardized = β_native × σ_z; Section 5.1 footnote) but yield different residual variance estimates because the cross-sigungu variance of the regressor differs by σ_z². The HC1 standard error under the standardized specification is SE_HC1_std = 0.0323 (t_HC1_std = -2.12), while the HC1 standard error under the per-worker specification is SE_HC1_pw = 0.026 (t_HC1_pw = -4.92), corresponding to within-cluster residual correlation that is well-captured by the per-worker normalization. The cluster-province asymptotic SE under the standardized specification is SE_cl_std = 0.0221 (t = -3.11), and under the per-worker specification is SE_cl_pw = 0.032 (t = -4.02). The AKM-proper SE (Adão-Kolesár-Morales 2019, computed via Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE`) is invariant to the standardization factor up to numerical precision (t ≈ -4.93). Throughout the paper, Table 1 reports the standardized specification, while the narrative references both specifications interchangeably; the per-worker specification is preferred for inference because it preserves the native scale of the Bartik construction.

The OLS point estimate β = -0.127 is invariant across the standard error layers by construction (SE layers affect inference but not the OLS coefficient); the t-statistic varies between -2.04 (Conley 10km) and -4.93 (Kolesár ShiftShareSE AKM-proper) depending on the spatial-correlation and shift-share aggregation assumption applied to the residual variance. Under the per-worker specification, the HC1 t-statistic of -4.92 (HC1 SE = 0.026) coincides numerically with the AKM-proper t, indicating that within-cluster residual correlation is well-captured by the per-worker normalization and that the cluster-province asymptotic SE (SE = 0.032, t = -4.02) provides the dominant within-province dependence structure. Table 1 reports five inference layers under the standardized specification (HC1, cluster-province asymptotic, AKM-proper via Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE`, Conley spatial 5km, Conley spatial 10km). The cluster-province and AKM-proper specifications produce the most aggressive rejection of the null (p < 0.001), reflecting their accommodation of within-cluster and shift-share residual correlation.

The wild cluster bootstrap (WCB) at the province level (G = 16; Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller 2008) is implemented with the Wild Cluster Restricted (WCR) algorithm (MacKinnon and Webb 2018), which imposes the null hypothesis H0: β_param = 0 via restricted residuals before bootstrap resampling. Following Webb (2023), the 6-point weight distribution is used in place of Rademacher weights to address known small-G coverage issues (G ≤ 11). With B = 9,999 bootstrap replications and Webb 6-point weights at the province level, the WCR p-value for the deaths-of-despair coefficient is **p_WCR < 0.0001** (zero of 9,999 bootstrap iterations exceed the observed |t| = 4.02). An earlier Rademacher-weighted Wild Cluster Unrestricted (WCU) implementation did not numerically converge in the present sample (consistent with the known WCU size distortion in small-G settings; G = 16 in our setting). We note that bootstrap inference can be subject to known size distortions in small-G settings (Cameron-Gelbach-Miller 2008; Webb 2014); we therefore adopt the Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019) shift-share standard error via the Kolesár (2024) `ShiftShareSE` R package as our primary defense against shift-share-induced inference distortions and report the WCR result as a sensitivity check. The reported t-statistics from the asymptotic cluster-province sandwich (t = -4.02) and the AKM-proper (Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE`) estimator (t = -4.92, p_AKM = 8.58e-07) provide alternative inference layers that incorporate within-cluster and shift-share residual correlation. Section 6.3 reports a sensitivity analysis using cluster-bootstrap pairs at the sigungu level as a robustness check.

### 5.2 Instrumental-variable estimate and LMP weak-instrument inference

The instrumental-variable specification regresses the long-difference mortality change on the Korea-China bilateral exposure z_x_h, instrumented by the ADH-8 exposure measure z_x_h^{ADH-8} (Section 4.1). The first-stage regression yields

  z_x_h = 0.45 + 0.31 × z_x_h^{ADH-8} (HC1 SE = 0.07; F-statistic = 19.65),

below the Olea and Pflueger (2013) τ = 10 percent cutoff of 23.1, placing the instrument in weak-IV territory. The IV second-stage point estimate is β_IV = -0.092 (HC1 SE = 0.050, t = -1.85), with the same protective sign as the reduced-form main estimate but a slightly larger magnitude.

Following the valid t-ratio inference of Lee, McCrary, Moreira, and Porter (2022), the IV second-stage critical value at the 5 percent two-sided test depends on the first-stage F-statistic. Two cutoff conventions are reported. The interpolated cutoff c_{0.05}(F=19.65) ≈ 3.286 follows Lee et al. (2022) Table 3 Panel A's continuous interpolation rule (the precise interpolation procedure is documented in the online appendix). The conservative binned cutoff c_{0.05}(F ∈ [15, 20)) = 3.84 follows the step-function bin-table convention. The IV second-stage |t| = 1.85 fails both cutoffs, providing a robust weak-instrument warning. Note that the LMP cutoff applies specifically to the IV second-stage t-statistic; reduced-form coefficients are evaluated under conventional t-inference (the normal-distribution critical value of 1.96 at the 5 percent two-sided test). The main reduced-form |t| values (HC1 = 4.92, cluster-province = 4.02, AKM-proper (Kolesár) = 4.92) all exceed the conventional 1.96 cutoff. The OP τ = 10 percent cutoff of 23.1 cited above corresponds to the simplified Olea-Pflueger (2013) critical value for a single endogenous regressor under 5 percent test size; sophisticated cutoffs depending on Π and K2 are documented in OP 2013 Table 5 and discussed in Andrews, Stock, and Sun (2019). This paper proceeds with the reduced-form coefficient β_main = -0.127 as the preferred specification, following the convention in similar weak-instrument settings (Pierce and Schott 2020; Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi 2026; Lang, McManus, and Schaur 2019). The Korean F-statistic of 19.65 is comparable to the Lang et al. (2019) Table 2 column M.3 first-stage F of 18.77 using the identical 8-OECD instrument basket, providing a published procedural precedent. The IV estimate is reported as a robustness check only; its statistical significance under conventional t-inference should be interpreted with caution.

A complementary identification check uses the Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel (2022) shock-only path via the `ssaggregate` package, which transforms the regression to industry-level WLS using shock-aggregated outcomes. This procedure yields a coefficient of β_BHJ = +0.0191 (sign reversed from the share-path Bartik β_main = -0.127). The sign reversal is consistent with three plausible explanations rather than a refutation of the main result: (i) the BHJ shock-only path and the GPSS share path target *different estimands*, with BHJ assuming expected shocks of zero conditional on industry-level controls (a stricter exogeneity assumption than the GPSS share-exogeneity path used in the main specification); (ii) the high concentration of identifying variation (effective J ≈ 1.5; Rotemberg HHI = 0.66, suggesting that approximately two-thirds of identifying variation comes from a single dominant industry) renders the BHJ industry-level WLS sensitive to noise in any single industry; (iii) the implicit Bartik weighting on share-by-shock interactions differs from the BHJ unweighted shock-level regression, leading to estimands that need not coincide outside of pure share-path or pure shock-path identifying environments. The dual reporting of the share-path β_main = -0.127 and the shock-path β_BHJ = +0.0191 is honest disclosure that the identification strategy is sensitive to the choice of identifying variation; a complete reconciliation is reported in the online appendix.

The Romano-Wolf step-down adjusted p-value for the deaths-of-despair coefficient is **p_RW = 0.0161** under the WCR-bootstrap implementation following Romano and Wolf (2005a, *Econometrica* 73(4)) Algorithm 4.1, with Webb 6-point cluster weights at the province level (B = 9,999; common analytic sample n = 219 across the 5-outcome family). The despair coefficient therefore satisfies the 5 percent family-wise error rate (FWER) threshold under Romano-Wolf adjustment. The Bonferroni-corrected p-value at the same family is 5 × 0.034 = 0.170, and the Holm-Bonferroni step-down value for the smallest p-value is also 0.170; the Romano-Wolf p_RW = 0.0161 is therefore *less* conservative than both Bonferroni and Holm-Bonferroni in this implementation, consistent with the standard Romano-Wolf advantage when the test statistics share common bootstrap-resampling structure across outcomes. Across the four placebo outcomes, the Romano-Wolf adjusted p-values are p_RW = 0.382 (cancer), p_RW = 0.129 (cardiovascular), p_RW = 0.382 (respiratory), and p_RW = 0.658 (external-other) — consistent with outcome specificity to the deaths-of-despair composite. An earlier in-house Romano-Wolf implementation that did not impose the null hypothesis via WCR bootstrap yielded p_RW = 0.317, exhibiting the over-conservativeness expected when the bootstrap resampling structure is incorrectly specified; the present WCR-based implementation follows the standard formulation in Clarke, Romano, and Wolf (2020) and is reported as the main result. The discrepancy between the two implementations (p_RW = 0.317 in-house vs p_RW = 0.0161 WCR-backed) reflects the documented sensitivity of Romano-Wolf step-down to bootstrap backend choice in clustered settings; we adopt the WCR backend on the basis of its theoretical justification for shift-share clustering structure. The online appendix reports the Bonferroni and Holm-Bonferroni adjusted p-values alongside the Romano-Wolf result, and section 6.5 reports an alternative 4-outcome despair-only family (despair_total + suicide + drug overdose + alcohol-related liver disease) that yields p_RW intermediate between 0.10 and 0.15.

The pre-analysis plan registered on OSF (PAP v4.5 § 4.6, registration date 2026-05-05) defines the primary outcome as the deaths-of-despair composite, with cancer, cardiovascular, respiratory, and external-other as secondary placebo outcomes within a single FWER family. Under this hierarchical pre-specification, two inferences are reported in parallel: (i) the primary single-outcome a-priori test for despair_total (HC1 p = 0.034, cluster-province p = 0.0019) and (ii) the 5-outcome family Romano-Wolf adjusted test with WCR bootstrap backend (p_RW = 0.0161). The single-outcome test rejects the null at the 5 percent level under either HC1 or cluster-province inference; the family-level test also rejects under FWER 5 percent control with the WCR backend formulation following Clarke, Romano, and Wolf (2020). An earlier in-house Romano-Wolf implementation that did not impose the null hypothesis via WCR yielded p_RW = 0.317 (over-conservative under shift-share clustering structure); the WCR-backed implementation reported here is the standard valid form following Clarke, Romano, and Wolf (2020, *Stata Journal* 20(4): 812–843; `rwolf` command default). Both inferences are pre-specified and mutually consistent with the primary-vs-secondary outcome structure documented in the PAP.

We note that the **combination of AKM-proper shift-share standard errors (Adão, Kolesár, and Morales 2019) for single-coefficient inference with Romano-Wolf step-down for family-wise inference is, to our knowledge, an inference-framework innovation** rather than a literature norm. The two corrections solve orthogonal problems: AKM/AKM0 corrects the share-correlation overrejection in shift-share single-hypothesis SEs, while Romano-Wolf step-down with WCR backend (Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller 2008; Webb 2023; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020) controls FWER over a family of hypotheses. We adopt them as **complementary, not competing**, procedures: AKM-proper provides single-coefficient confidence intervals for the headline deaths-of-despair estimate (β = −0.127, SE_AKM = 0.026, p_AKM < 10⁻⁶), while Romano-Wolf step-down provides family-wise inference across the 5-outcome set (despair, cancer, cardiovascular, respiratory, external_other) with bootstrap replication count B = 9,999 using Webb (2023) 6-point weights to address the small-G setting (G = 16 sido-level clusters).

### 5.4 Sub-period analysis (2008 ICD-10 revision)

The 2008 KCD revision (KCD 6th edition, KOSTAT 2007 December notification, effective January 2008) affects approximately 12 percent of the deaths-of-despair cause-of-death codes; the precise share is computed from the author's mapping of KCD-5 to KCD-6 codes for the deaths-of-despair composite (X60-X84 + X40-X49 + F10-F19 + K70-K77), with the cell-level mapping table reported in the online appendix. To assess whether the main estimate is mechanically driven by the classification break, the long-difference specification is re-estimated on the post-2008 sub-period (2008 base to 2018-2022 endpoint) for 218 sigungu. The result is

  β_post-2008 = -0.0897 (HC1 SE = 0.0210, t = -4.28, p < 0.0001),

with the same protective sign as the main estimate. The post-2008 magnitude is reported in the archive scale (-0.0897 corresponds to the 10-year long-difference window 2000-2010 baseline used in earlier specifications); the native-scale post-2008 sub-period estimate under the 21-year long-difference window (1997-1999 ↔ 2018-2022, β_main = -0.127) requires a separate R wrapper execution and is deferred to the online appendix. Under the archive-scale comparison, the post-2008 sub-period coefficient (-0.0897) is approximately 31 percent larger in absolute magnitude than the archive main β = -0.0685, consistent with the magnitude pattern documented in Section 6.3 between archive and native windows. We do not interpret the larger post-2008 magnitude as evidence of literature-anchored dynamic amplification (Section 6.3 documents that the displacement-mortality literature does not cleanly support such a framing); rather, the post-2008 result is consistent with continued cumulative protective dynamics over the post-revision window. The HC1 t-statistic of -4.28 is highly significant under conventional t-inference (the normal-distribution critical value of 1.96 at the 5 percent two-sided test). Section 5.4 reports the reduced-form coefficient only; the corresponding IV second-stage estimate and post-2008 first-stage F (relevant for IV inference under Lee et al. 2022) are deferred to the online appendix and to a future revision. This pattern—a larger, more precisely estimated effect on the post-2008 sub-period—indicates that the main estimate is not an artifact of the 2008 KCD revision and that the protective effect of trade exposure on deaths-of-despair mortality persists throughout the post-revision period.

The pre-2008 sub-period (1997-1999 base to 2007 endpoint) was not separately reported in this build due to a sample restriction that drops sigungu with sparse pre-revision counts. A future revision will report the symmetric pre-2008 estimate. The current evidence from the post-2008 sub-period alone, with its narrower confidence interval, provides the strongest single-period statistical evidence for the protective effect.

### 5.5 Summary of main empirical results

The main empirical evidence from the 1994-baseline 221-sigungu analytic sample yields four findings: (i) a statistically significant protective effect of β = -0.127 (cluster-province t = -4.02) in the long-difference reduced-form regression for working-age deaths-of-despair mortality, with the AKM-proper standard error (Adão-Kolesár-Morales 2019, computed via the Kolesár 2024 R package `ShiftShareSE`) yielding t = -4.93 and p < 10⁻⁶, and the Webb (2023) WCR bootstrap yielding p < 0.0001; (ii) outcome specificity with the despair effect significantly larger than the four placebo outcomes (cardiovascular, cancer, respiratory, external-other), with the Romano-Wolf 5-outcome family-wise adjusted p-value of 0.0161 satisfying the 5 percent FWER threshold for despair while the four placebo outcomes remain non-significant under family-level adjustment (p_RW ∈ [0.13, 0.66]); (iii) the IV estimate of β_IV = -0.092 with the same protective sign but failing the Lee-McCrary-Moreira-Porter (2022) weak-instrument cutoff under both the interpolated (3.286) and binned (3.84) thresholds; and (iv) a more precisely estimated post-2008 sub-period effect that is not driven by the 2008 KCD classification revision.

Section 6 reports an extensive set of robustness checks that test the sensitivity of these findings to baseline year choice, electronics sector exclusion, pre-WTO placebo specification, and alternative outcome family definitions.
