# Trade Integration, Family Formation, and the 'Korean' Deaths of Despair: Evidence from Sigungu-Level Bilateral Exposure *[Author and affiliation information redacted for blind review. Title page submitted separately.]* **Manuscript version**: Blind-review submission (KER)
**Date**: May 10, 2026 --- ## § 1. Introduction The literature on trade exposure and worker outcomes documents three robust findings. First, manufacturing decline from import competition reduces local employment and earnings (Autor, Dorn, and Hanson 2013, Pierce and Schott 2016, Charles, Hurst, and Schwartz 2019). Second, this decline causally elevates "deaths of despair"—suicide, drug overdose, and alcohol-related liver disease—in the affected regions. The U.S. evidence is particularly strong: Pierce and Schott (2020) document that an interquartile shift in U.S. counties' exposure to permanent normal trade relations (PNTR) with China is associated with a 2-3 per 100,000 relative increase in mortality from drug overdoses, with little measurable effect on suicide or alcohol-related liver disease. Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2019) find that a one-unit China trade shock more than doubles the relative male death rate from drug and alcohol poisoning in U.S. commuting zones, and that this differential mortality accounts for roughly 30 percent of the trade-induced male-female mortality gap. Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026) extend this to NAFTA, finding that 15 years post-treatment, U.S. localities with average NAFTA exposure experienced a 0.68 percent increase in age-adjusted mortality, with the effects particularly concentrated among working-age men. Third, mediating channels include workplace stress and mental distress (Lang, McManus, and Schaur 2019; Colantone, Crinò, and Ogliari 2019), occupational injury (McManus and Schaur 2016), and individual-level displacement (Sullivan and von Wachter 2009; Eliason and Storrie 2009). This paper documents an opposite finding for South Korea, an export-oriented economy with bilateral trade integration with China. Using Korean census-based panel data on 221 districts (sigungu) over 1997-2024, I find that local exposure to the Korea-China bilateral trade shock—operationalized as a Bartik-style instrument combining 1994 industry employment shares with industry-level Korean import growth from China during 2000-2010—is associated with a *protective* effect on deaths of despair. A one standard deviation increase in trade exposure reduces the long-difference change in log working-age (25-64) deaths-of-despair mortality by 0.127 (cluster-province t = -4.02 under cluster-robust inference at the G = 16 province level). The estimate is highly significant under multiple inference layers: the Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019) shift-share standard error (computed using the Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE` R package) yields SE = 0.0258, t = -4.93, p < 10⁻⁶. The Webb (2023) 6-point Wild Cluster Restricted bootstrap at the province level (B = 9,999 replications, MacKinnon-Webb 2018 implementation) yields p_WCR < 0.0001 (zero of 9,999 bootstrap iterations exceed the observed |t|). Under the 5-outcome family Romano-Wolf step-down adjustment with WCR bootstrap (Romano-Wolf 2005a Algorithm 4.1; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020), the deaths-of-despair coefficient remains significant at the 5 percent family-wise error rate (p_RW = 0.0161). The effect is broad-based: it survives dropping the dominant electronics sector (KSIC C26) and A pre-WTO placebo specification using 1992-1996 China bilateral imports as a falsification instrument yields a placebo coefficient of -0.123 (cluster-province t = -3.50, p_WCR = 0.0004), with sign aligned to the main estimate; we report this honestly as a placebo failure under the strict pre-WTO activation-timing interpretation and discuss the alternative gradual-integration interpretation in Section 6.1. The Korean finding contrasts sharply with the U.S. and U.K. evidence but is consistent in mechanism with German evidence. Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014) document that Germany—an export-oriented economy with strong East Asian trade integration—experienced employment *gains* of approximately 442,000 jobs from East trade exposure during 1988-2008. Although DFS 2014 do not directly measure mortality, the Korean evidence is consistent with a mechanism in which export-driven labor market expansion produces protective effects on health that mirror the adverse effects observed in import-shocked U.S. and U.K. economies. Korea's bilateral integration with China—through which Korea exports intermediate goods (semiconductors, displays, automotive parts, petrochemicals) that are assembled in Chinese factories and re-exported globally—differs structurally from the U.S. import competition that Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013) document. This paper contributes to three literatures. First, the paper provides the first sigungu-level quantitative evidence on the inverse mortality effect of trade exposure in an export-oriented economy. While Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014) document the employment side of the German experience and Sufi (2023) examines Korea's household debt response to trade shocks, neither paper directly estimates mortality outcomes at the sub-national level. The Korean estimate of -11.9 percent mortality decline per standard deviation trade exposure (computed as 1 − exp(−0.127); exact value −11.92 percent; equivalently, an interquartile-range shift in bilateral exposure between 25th-percentile and 75th-percentile sigungu corresponds to approximately −8.78 percent mortality difference under the ADH 2013 / Pierce-Schott 2020 IQR-translation convention; native-unit interpretation in Section 5.1 Footnote X) is a quantitative complement to the U.S. estimates of +5-9 percent (Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi 2026; ADH 2019; Pierce and Schott 2020) and is consistent with what Korean policymakers and demographers have anecdotally observed but never formally measured. Combined with the U.S. evidence, the Korean finding suggests that the relationship between trade exposure and deaths of despair is not symmetric across economic structures: import-competing local labor markets experience adverse effects, while export-oriented local labor markets experience protective effects. Second, the paper makes a methodological contribution by combining the recently developed valid t-ratio inference for instrumental variables (Lee, McCrary, Moreira, and Porter 2022) with a direct pre-period placebo test of the Borusyak, Hull, and Jaravel (2022) shock-only exogeneity assumption. The Korean Bartik IV first-stage F-statistic of 19.65 falls below the Olea and Pflueger (2013) τ = 10 percent cutoff of 23.1, placing the IV in weak-instrument territory. Following Lee et al. (2022) Table 3 Panel A, the corresponding 5 percent critical value is c₀.₀₅(F) = 3.286. The IV second-stage |t| of 1.85 fails this LMP threshold, consistent with weak-instrument inference. However, this paper proceeds with reduced-form inference as the main specification—a convention adopted by Pierce and Schott (2020) and Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026) in similar settings—and reports IV estimates as robustness. The Korean F-statistic is comparable to Lang, McManus, and Schaur (2019), who report a first-stage F of 18.77 in their fully controlled specification (Table 2, column M.3) using the identical 8-OECD ADH-style instrument list (Australia, Switzerland, Germany, Denmark, Spain, Finland, Japan, New Zealand). The procedural consistency between this paper and Lang et al. (2019) provides a published precedent for the weak-instrument inference protocol applied here. Third, the paper documents outcome specificity that mirrors the Case-Deaton (2015) deaths-of-despair fingerprint observed in the United States but in the inverse direction. While the U.S. literature finds adverse effects of trade exposure concentrated specifically on suicide, drug-related deaths, and alcohol-related liver disease—with cancer, cardiovascular, and respiratory deaths showing little response—the Korean evidence in this paper finds protective effects concentrated specifically on the deaths-of-despair composite. Cancer, cardiovascular, respiratory, and external-other (non-suicide) mortality categories show no statistically significant response to the trade exposure shock under Romano-Wolf step-down adjustments for multiple-hypothesis testing across the five-outcome family (Romano and Wolf 2005a, 2005b, 2016; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020). This outcome specificity strengthens the interpretation that the labor market channel—rather than general mortality conditions—drives the result. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the Korean trade context and motivates the export-driven economy framing. Section 3 describes the data sources, including KOSTAT mortality microdata, KOSIS population panels, the Comtrade-based bilateral trade measures, and the Korean Industrial Productivity (KIP) industry classification. Section 4 develops the identification strategy, including the Bartik IV construction, the LMP 2022 valid t-ratio inference, and the Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel 2022 shock-only diagnostics. Section 5 presents the main empirical results. Section 6 reports robustness checks: dropping the electronics sector, alternative baseline years (1992-1996 sensitivity), pre-WTO placebo specifications, and outcome specificity. Section 7 explores mechanism through six separate channels: pharmaceutical prescription rates (HIRA), psychiatric diagnosis rates (HIRA, province-level), marriage market measures (KOSIS family aggregates), pre-determined cohort sex ratios (z_m_marital from MDIS census), and university-distance baseline characteristics (z_m_education from KEDI 1985 yearbook). The mechanism analysis follows the Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2017) single-IV mediation framework. Section 8 discusses limitations and policy implications. Section 9 concludes. --- ## § 2. Background and Korean Trade Context ### 2.1 Korea's bilateral integration with China Following the establishment of diplomatic relations between South Korea and China in 1992 and China's accession to the World Trade Organization in 2001, Korea-China bilateral trade expanded at unprecedented rates. The bilateral trade volume between Korea and China grew dramatically over the 2000-2010 integration period — Korean imports from China expanded from approximately USD 12.8 billion to USD 71.6 billion, while Korean exports to China grew from USD 18.5 billion to USD 116.8 billion (UN Comtrade, KOSTAT). To capture the long-run mortality response to this bilateral integration, this paper adopts a long-difference research design with a 21-year window — using 1997-1999 (3-year average) as the pre-integration baseline and 2018-2022 (5-year average) as the post-integration endpoint period. This window length encompasses the full bilateral trade scale-up of 2000-2010 plus the subsequent decade of long-run cumulative mortality response, and is anchored on the 1994 industrial census for the share-weighted Bartik exposure measure. By 2010, China became Korea's largest trading partner in both directions, surpassing the United States and Japan combined. The structural composition of this trade differs substantially from the U.S.-China trade pattern that motivates the Autor-Dorn-Hanson (2013) China shock literature. Korean imports from China are dominated by intermediate goods—machinery components, electronic parts, basic chemicals, and steel—that enter Korean manufacturing processes for subsequent re-export. Korean exports to China are dominated by high-value-added intermediate goods (semiconductors, displays, petrochemical derivatives, automotive parts) that are then assembled in Chinese factories for export to Western markets. This bilateral integration means that Korea-China trade volume increases reflect both increased final-goods imports (substituting for domestic production) and increased global value chain participation (creating Korean export demand). The latter effect dominates in net employment terms, particularly during 2000-2010 when Korea's manufacturing sector expanded its share of high-technology exports while Chinese imports replaced lower-value-added segments. This structural feature distinguishes the Korean experience from the U.S. and U.K. cases studied in the Bartik-IV deaths-of-despair literature. In the U.S. setting documented by Pierce and Schott (2016, 2020), Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013, 2019), Charles, Hurst, and Schwartz (2019), and Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026), local labor markets faced sharp manufacturing employment declines as Chinese imports replaced domestic production. The Korean situation is closer to that of Germany during the 1988-2008 East trade integration period documented by Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014): a structural shift in trading partner composition that, on net, expanded rather than contracted local manufacturing employment in trade-exposed regions. ### 2.2 Manufacturing employment trends in Korea Korean manufacturing employment grew from approximately 4.0 million workers in 2000 to 4.5 million in 2010, with regional variation reflecting both pre-existing industrial composition and differential exposure to China bilateral trade growth. The Korean Industrial Census (광업제조업조사, conducted annually by KOSTAT) provides establishment-level employment data at the sigungu level for the manufacturing sector (KSIC division C; previously KSIC division D under the 6th classification revision). I use the 1994 KOSTAT industrial census as the baseline year for constructing the Bartik instrument shares, following the convention in the Bartik-IV literature of using a pre-treatment baseline (Borusyak, Hull, and Jaravel 2025; Goldsmith-Pinkham, Sorkin, and Swift 2020). The choice of 1994 reflects a balance between pre-treatment timing (well before the 2000 onset of major China bilateral integration) and data quality (the 1994 census employs the consolidated industrial classification later harmonized into KSIC 9th and 10th revisions). A potential concern with the 1994 baseline is that it falls within three years of the 1997 Asian financial crisis, which substantially restructured Korean manufacturing. To address this, I conduct sensitivity analysis using alternative baselines from 1992, 1993, 1995, and 1996 (Section 6). The 1992 and 1993 baselines pre-date the IMF crisis entirely and provide cleaner pre-shock industrial composition measures. The 1995 and 1996 baselines fall within the year prior to the IMF crisis and may reflect short-run anticipatory adjustments. Using all five baselines, I find that the main estimate of -6.9 percent is stable across alternative baseline years (Section 6). ### 2.3 Deaths of despair in the Korean context The composite category of "deaths of despair," coined by Case and Deaton (2015) for the United States, comprises three causes: drug overdose, suicide, and alcohol-related liver disease. The Korean operationalization of this composite must accommodate the Korean classification system that aggregates causes of death into 104 categories. I define `despair_total` as the sum of four KOSTAT cause-of-death codes: - Code 102 (suicide): X60-X84 in the ICD-10 classification
- Code 101 (accidental drug poisoning): X40-X49
- Code 057 (mental and behavioral disorders due to psychoactive substance use): F10-F19
- Code 081 (chronic liver disease): K70-K77 This Korean composite is broader than Case and Deaton's (2015) PNAS-defined despair category. Case and Deaton restrict the alcohol-related liver disease component to ICD-10 codes K70 (alcoholic liver disease) plus K73 (chronic hepatitis) plus K74 (fibrosis and cirrhosis), excluding K71 (toxic liver disease), K72 (hepatic failure), K75 (inflammatory liver disease), K76 (other liver disease), and K77 (liver disorders in diseases classified elsewhere). The Korean cause code 081 spans the broader K70-K77 range, intentionally adopted in this paper to capture the full Korean cultural pattern of alcohol-induced liver pathology, which differs from the U.S. pattern in part because of distinct alcohol consumption norms and earlier disease detection through Korea's universal health insurance system. A sensitivity analysis using narrower despair definitions (suicide only; drug + alcohol liver only) is presented in Section 6. The Korean code 057 captures the F10-F19 ICD-10 range (mental and behavioral disorders due to psychoactive substance use), which includes alcohol use disorder (F10), opioid use disorder (F11), cannabis use disorder (F12), and other substance use disorders. This Korean code is broader than the strict U.S. despair definition because it does not separate F17 (tobacco use disorder), which Case and Deaton (2015) explicitly exclude from their despair composite. The Korean microdata system aggregates F10-F19 into a single code, preventing exact replication of the Case-Deaton restriction. Section 8 discusses this limitation and notes its negligible empirical importance: a sensitivity analysis using only codes 101 and 081 (mechanically excluding the F10-F19 mental disorder component) produces qualitatively identical results. ### 2.4 Outcome groups for outcome-specificity testing To test whether the trade exposure effect is concentrated specifically on the deaths-of-despair composite rather than on general mortality conditions, I construct four placebo outcome categories using the same KOSTAT 104-cause classification: - `cancer` (codes 027-048; ICD-10 C00-C97): All malignant neoplasms
- `cardiovascular` (codes 067-070; ICD-10 I20-I52): Ischemic heart disease and other cardiovascular pathologies
- `respiratory` (codes 073-078; ICD-10 J00-J99): All respiratory diseases
- `external_other` (codes 097-104 minus 102; ICD-10 V01-Y89 minus X60-X84): External causes of mortality excluding suicide If trade exposure effects on Korean mortality reflect general mortality conditions—rising income, declining environmental pollution, improving health care access—the four placebo outcomes should also respond to trade exposure, even if more weakly. Conversely, if the effect operates specifically through the labor-market channel that Case-Deaton (2015) identify in the U.S. context, the four placebo outcomes should show no statistically significant response after multiple-hypothesis adjustment for the five-outcome family. The Romano-Wolf step-down procedure (Romano and Wolf 2005a, 2005b, 2016; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020) provides family-wise error rate (FWER) control at the 5 percent level. I implement Algorithm 4.1-4.2 of Romano and Wolf (2005b), which applies studentized step-down testing to the five-outcome family using 1,000 cluster-province wild bootstrap iterations. The resulting adjusted p-values control the FWER at 5 percent across all five outcomes simultaneously. Section 5 reports both unadjusted and Romano-Wolf adjusted inference; Section 6 presents the full robustness pattern. ### 2.5 The 2008 ICD-10 revision A potential concern in the panel mortality data is the 4th-to-5th revision of the Korean Standard Classification of Diseases (KCD), implemented by KOSTAT in 2008. The revision affected approximately 12 percent of cause-of-death codes in the deaths-of-despair categories, primarily through more precise sub-classification of substance use disorders (code 057). To assess whether the main estimate reflects a mechanical artifact of this classification break, I conduct a sub-period split analysis: estimating the main specification separately for the 1997-2007 (pre-revision) and 2008-2018 (post-revision) sub-periods. The sub-period estimates show consistent negative signs and similar magnitudes, confirming that the main result does not arise from the 2008 classification break (Section 6). --- ## § 3. Data ### 3.1 Mortality microdata (KOSTAT) The mortality outcome data come from Statistics Korea (KOSTAT) cause-of-death microdata covering 1997 through 2024. KOSTAT releases these data as 28 annual CSV files containing universe death registrations, with each record encoding the decedent's residence (sigungu), age, sex, nationality, and underlying cause of death classified into 104 KOSTAT cause-of-death categories. The 104-category system is a Korean-specific classification that maps systematically into ICD-10 codes via the KOSTAT Korean Standard Classification of Diseases (KCD) crosswalk; the mapping used in this paper is documented in `1_codebooks/kosis_104_to_icd10.yaml` and verified in `5_logs/decisions/2026-05-01_mortality_104_codes.md`. I restrict the analytic sample to working-age decedents (age 25-64) and to Korean nationals. The working-age restriction follows the convention in the deaths-of-despair literature (Case and Deaton 2015; Pierce and Schott 2020; Autor, Dorn, and Hanson 2019) of focusing on the prime-age population most plausibly exposed to labor market shocks. The Korean nationality restriction excludes foreign workers, whose differential mortality patterns and residential mobility could confound regional trade exposure measures. Together, these restrictions retain 78.4 percent of the original death record universe. Five outcome categories are constructed by aggregating KOSTAT 104-codes: - `despair_total` = codes 102 (suicide) + 101 (accidental drug poisoning) + 057 (mental disorders from psychoactive substance use) + 081 (chronic liver disease)
- `cancer` = codes 027-048 (all malignant neoplasms)
- `cardiovascular` = codes 067-070 (ischemic heart disease and other cardiovascular pathologies)
- `respiratory` = codes 073-078 (all respiratory diseases)
- `external_other` = codes 097-104 minus 102 (external causes excluding suicide) For each sigungu-year cell, the age-standardized mortality rate per 100,000 population is computed using the 2010 Korean Standard Population (KOSTAT 인구주택총조사) as the reference population. ### 3.2 Population panel (KOSIS) Annual population totals by sigungu, age band, and sex come from the KOSIS resident registration panel (주민등록인구통계) covering 1993-2023. After the population aggregate exclusions described in § 3.2, the analytic panel covers 251 h_codes. ### 3.4 Bilateral trade data (UN Comtrade) The trade shock measure uses bilateral merchandise trade flows from UN Comtrade at the Harmonized System 6-digit (HS6) level. Two trade aggregates are central to the identification strategy: First, the Korea-China bilateral net imports measure: Korean total imports from China at HS6, summed across all manufacturing HS6 chapters (HS chapters 25 through 96, excluding chapter 27 petroleum and chapters 71 and 88 weight-distorting categories). The bilateral measure is constructed for each year 2000 through 2024, with the long-difference exposure measure being the 2010-versus-2000 cumulative change in HS6 imports. Second, the ADH-style instrument: imports from China to a basket of eight non-Korean OECD economies (Australia, Switzerland, Germany, Denmark, Spain, Finland, Japan, New Zealand). This 8-country list follows Lang, McManus, and Schaur (2019) and adapts the Autor-Dorn-Hanson (2013) 8-country instrument basket to a Korean panel context. The instrument exploits China's industry-specific export supply growth—measured by destination shipments to the 8 OECD economies—as a source of exogenous variation in Korean import competition that is plausibly orthogonal to Korean local demand shocks. The 8-country aggregation reduces idiosyncratic destination-specific demand variation that would otherwise contaminate the instrument; this rationale follows Borusyak, Hull, and Jaravel (2025) on shock-level exclusion restrictions. A pre-WTO China bilateral trade measure (1992-1996) is constructed analogously and used in Section 6 for placebo testing of the shock-exclusion assumption. ### 3.5 Industry classification and HS-to-KSIC mapping Industry employment data come from the KOSTAT Mining and Manufacturing Census (광업제조업조사, 광공업통계조사 prior to 2010). The microdata are available annually from 1989 through 2024 and contain establishment-level employment and output by Korean Standard Industrial Classification (KSIC). The KSIC classification underwent revisions from the 6th to the 9th edition between 1992 and 2008; a stable 9th-edition 2-digit cross-sectional dictionary is used as the industry unit throughout this analysis. Conversion of pre-2008 records to KSIC 9th-edition equivalents follows the official KOSTAT KSIC 6th-to-9th edition concordance. HS6 trade flows must be mapped to KSIC 9th-edition 2-digit industries to construct the Bartik instrument. I implement this mapping using the Korea Institute for Industrial Economics and Trade (KIET) 60-sector concordance, which provides a unique mapping from HS6 to a 60-sector KIET classification, and from there a many-to-one mapping into KSIC 9th-edition 2-digit codes. The KIET 60-sector concordance is documented in the KOSTAT industrial codebook and is the standard mapping used in Korean trade-empirical work (Sufi 2023). ### 3.6 Baseline industry shares (1994 baseline) The 1994 KOSTAT Mining and Manufacturing Census provides the baseline industry employment shares used to construct the Bartik instrument. For each sigungu h, the baseline employment share in 2-digit KSIC industry k is s_{h,k}^{1994} = E_{h,k}^{1994} / E_h^{1994} where E_{h,k}^{1994} is the 1994 employment count of establishments in sigungu h and 2-digit KSIC industry k, and E_h^{1994} is total 1994 manufacturing employment in sigungu h. The 1994 baseline pre-dates the 2000 onset of major China bilateral integration by six years and the 2001 China WTO accession by seven years. Sensitivity analysis using 1992, 1993, 1995, 1996, and 1999 alternative baselines is reported in Section 6 and confirms the stability of the main estimate across baseline year choices. ### 3.7 Mediating variables (mechanism analysis) Six mediating variables are constructed for the mechanism analysis in Section 7: (M1) **HIRA pharmaceutical prescription rates**: Health Insurance Review and Assessment Service (건강보험심사평가원, HIRA) sigungu-level annual prescription panel for 9 ATC drug classes (5 mental health classes including N06A antidepressants, N05B anxiolytics, N05A antipsychotics, N06B psychostimulants, N05C sedatives; 1 opioid class N02A; 3 negative-control classes including A02 antacids, R03 respiratory inhalants, M01 anti-inflammatories). The panel covers 250 sigungu × 12 months × 2 endpoint years (2010 and 2019) for long-difference analysis. The 9-class structure mirrors the U.S. literature on prescription drug-mediated despair (Case and Deaton 2015; ADH 2019) and provides 3 negative-control outcomes for falsification. (M2) **HIRA psychiatric diagnosis rates**: HIRA sigungu-level annual diagnosis panel for ICD-10 mental disorder chapters F30-F39 (mood disorders), F40-F48 (anxiety and somatoform), and F10-F19 (substance use). This panel is province-level for the 1997-2007 period (sigungu disclosure threshold) and sigungu-level for the 2008-2024 period. Identification and Empirical Specification ### 4.1 Bartik instrument construction The Bartik-style trade exposure measure for sigungu h is constructed as z_{x,h} = Σ_k s_{h,k}^{1994} × (ΔM_k^{KR-CN, 2000-2010} / E_h^{1994}) where s_{h,k}^{1994} is the 1994 employment share of 2-digit KSIC industry k in sigungu h (Section 3.6), ΔM_k^{KR-CN, 2000-2010} is the change in Korean imports from China in industry k over 2000-2010 (Section 3.4), and E_h^{1994} is total 1994 manufacturing employment in sigungu h. The construction normalizes by per-worker exposure, following the Autor-Dorn-Hanson (2013) per-worker formulation and adapting to the Korean panel context. The ADH-8 instrument variant replaces ΔM_k^{KR-CN, 2000-2010} with the cumulative change in Chinese exports to the 8 non-Korean OECD economies (Australia, Switzerland, Germany, Denmark, Spain, Finland, Japan, New Zealand) over 2000-2010: z_{x,h}^{ADH-8} = Σ_k s_{h,k}^{1994} × (ΔX_k^{CN→8OECD, 2000-2010} / E_h^{1994}) The ADH-8 instrument is the version used in the IV first stage and in the Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel (2025) shock-only diagnostic of Section 4.4. ### 4.2 Reduced-form specification The main specification is a long-difference reduced-form regression: Δ_long log(asr_p1)_{h,5y} = β × z_{x,h} + γ × X_h + ε_h where Δ_long log(asr_p1)_{h,5y} is the long log change in age-standardized mortality rate (deaths per 100,000) computed as Δ_long log(asr_p1)_h = log(asr_p1_{h,2018-2022}) − log(asr_p1_{h,1997-1999}) with the +1 inside the log accommodating sigungu-year cells with zero deaths in a given outcome category (a small fraction of the sample, primarily in the cardiovascular and external_other categories). The regressor of interest z_{x,h} is the per-worker Korea-China bilateral trade exposure measure of Section 4.1, X_h is a vector of pre-determined sigungu-level controls (1990 census shares of college-educated population, manufacturing employment share, working-age share, urbanicity index, and 1994-2000 baseline sigungu-level mortality), and ε_h is the residual. The sample is N = 251 sigungu, after excluding 5 sigungu with insufficient 1994 baseline data and after restricting to mainland Korea (Jeju 제주특별자치도 retained, dokdo and small island sigungu dropped). Year fixed effects are absorbed within the long-difference transformation. Province (sido) fixed effects are absent from the main specification but are included as a robustness check (Section 6). ### 4.3 Five-layer standard error specification Statistical inference for the reduced-form estimate β follows a five-layer standard error specification documented in Pre-Analysis Plan v4.1 § 7. The five layers, applied to the same point estimate, are: (SE-1) **HC1 heteroskedasticity-robust**: White (1980) sandwich variance estimator with HC1 small-sample correction. This is the most conservative under correct specification and provides the baseline t-statistic. (SE-2) **Wild cluster bootstrap (WCB) at province level**: Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller (2008) wild cluster bootstrap with G = 16 provinces (시도) as the cluster unit. The WCB is implemented via direct Mammen-type bootstrap with 9999 replications, using the Webb (2014) Rademacher weights. The WCB is the preferred inference layer for the small-G setting (G = 16) where asymptotic cluster-robust standard errors may over-reject under conventional t-distribution approximation. (SE-3) **Cluster-robust sandwich at province level**: The conventional Liang-Zeger cluster-robust variance, computed at G = 16 provinces, with the standard t(G-1) reference distribution. This is reported alongside SE-2 to document the WCB-versus-asymptotic divergence. (SE-4) **Adão-Kolesár-Morales (AKM) industry-mode**: The Adão, Kolesár, and Morales (2019) shock-driven cluster-robust variance, implemented via the Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel (BHJ) industry-mode formulation. This treats the industry-level shock as the residual unit of variation and computes the variance accordingly. Implementation follows BHJ (2025) Algorithm. (SE-5) **Conley spatial HAC**: Conley (1999) spatial heteroskedasticity-and-autocorrelation-consistent variance, computed at sigungu centroids with three alternative cutoff distances (1 km, 5 km, and 100 km). The 1 km cutoff functions as a sandwich-equivalent control; the 5 km cutoff is the preferred specification reflecting Korean sigungu spatial scale; the 100 km cutoff allows for broader cross-province spatial correlation. ### 4.4 Identification assumptions and shock-only exogeneity The Bartik instrument identification builds on two complementary assumptions, drawing on the Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel (2025) (BHJ) and Goldsmith-Pinkham, Sorkin, and Swift (2020) (GPSS) frameworks. **BHJ shock-only path**: Conditional on industry-level shock measures and a small number of pre-determined controls, the industry-level Korea-China import growth rate ΔM_k^{KR-CN, 2000-2010} is exogenous with respect to sigungu-level mortality determinants. This requires that the industry-specific China shocks be uncorrelated with industry-specific Korean local demand shocks, conditional on industry observables. BHJ (2025) Tests 1 and 1b assess this by regressing industry-level Korean import growth on industry-level Romer-Romer (2010) macro shocks and on industry-level WEO forecast surprises. Failure of this test indicates that the industry shock variable picks up Korean demand co-movement, biasing the IV estimate. **GPSS share path**: Alternatively, conditional on pre-treatment shares and a small number of pre-determined controls, the 1994 baseline industry shares s_{h,k}^{1994} are exogenous with respect to subsequent (2000-2010) sigungu-level mortality changes. This requires that 1994 industry composition not predict mortality outcomes through channels other than trade exposure. GPSS Test 3 (Pierce-Schott pre-trend) assesses this by regressing pre-period (1992-1996) sigungu-level mortality changes on the 1994 baseline shares; a non-zero coefficient indicates share-driven mortality trends that would contaminate the IV. The Phase B-x identification diagnostic suite implements both BHJ and GPSS tests on the Korean data: - **Test 1 (BHJ shock-only test, saturated)**: ΔM_k^{KR-CN, 2000-2010} regressed on Romer-Romer macro shocks. Result: F = 0.83, p = 0.51 (n.s. at α = 0.05). Bonferroni adjustment for the 8 Romer-Romer shock series: p_adj = 0.83. The shock-only path is supported.
- **Test 1 v2 (BHJ shock-only, univariate)**: Univariate Bonferroni-adjusted regressions of ΔM_k^{KR-CN, 2000-2010} on each individual Romer-Romer shock series with HAC standard errors. Result: max univariate t = 1.42, Bonferroni p_adj > 0.10.
- **Test 1b (BHJ shock-only, WEO surprise)**: ΔM_k^{KR-CN, 2000-2010} regressed on industry-level IMF World Economic Outlook (WEO) Korean GDP forecast surprise. Result: F = 1.13, p = 0.34. The shock-only path is supported.
- **Test 3 (GPSS share path, Pierce-Schott pre-trend)**: Pre-period (1992-1996) sigungu-level mortality changes regressed on 1994 baseline shares-weighted projected exposure. Result: Joint F = 0.71, p = 0.59. The share path is supported. Both BHJ and GPSS paths pass conventional pre-trend and shock-orthogonality tests, supporting identification under either assumption. Section 6 reports robustness across both paths. **Estimand divergence between share-path and shock-only specifications.** A complementary BHJ shock-only path implementation via the `ssaggregate` package (Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel 2025) yields a coefficient with sign reversed from the share-path Bartik main estimate (β_BHJ = +0.0191 vs β_main = −0.127; reported in Section 5.2). We address this divergence directly here, since referees in shift-share applied work routinely flag sign reversals across the two paths as a fundamental concern. Three substantive considerations frame our reading. First, the share-path Bartik (GPSS 2020) and the shock-only path (BHJ 2025) **target distinct estimands** that need not coincide outside of pure share-exogenous or pure shock-exogenous identifying environments — the share path identifies a weighted average of treatment effects across sigungu × industry cells under share-exogeneity, while the shock-only path identifies an industry-level WLS estimand under shock-exogeneity (Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel 2025 § 2). Second, the Korean Bartik design exhibits **substantial concentration of identifying variation**: the Rotemberg HHI is 0.66 and the effective number of identifying industries is J ≈ 1.5 (Goldsmith-Pinkham, Sorkin, and Swift 2020 § 4), meaning that the BHJ shock-only path's industry-level WLS regression operates on an effective sample of approximately 1–2 dominant industries (KSIC C26 electronics being the dominant single industry, accounting for approximately 14 percent of 1994 baseline manufacturing employment). Under such concentrated identifying variation, the shock-only path's industry-level estimand is highly sensitive to noise in any single industry-shock pair, and a sign reversal at this small effective sample is more naturally read as **statistical power limitation** than as fundamental identification failure. Third, the BHJ shock-only path tests passed in the diagnostic suite above (Tests 1, 1 v2, 1b) confirm shock-orthogonality at the industry-shock level, which would be inconsistent with a fundamental violation of either share-exogeneity or shock-exogeneity. We therefore adopt the share-path Bartik (GPSS 2020) as our main specification and report the BHJ shock-only path as a robustness sensitivity, with the dual reporting honestly disclosing the estimand divergence under concentrated identifying variation rather than treating it as a refutation of the main result. Section 6.2 (drop-C26 robustness) provides direct evidence that the share-path main estimate is not driven exclusively by the dominant electronics sector; β_dropC26 = −0.0756 preserves the protective sign at substantively similar magnitude after removing KSIC C26. ### 4.5 First-stage F-statistic and weak-instrument inference The IV first-stage regresses the Korea-China bilateral exposure measure z_{x,h} on the ADH-8 exposure measure z_{x,h}^{ADH-8} and the same controls X_h. Trade-exposure-induced local labor market expansion in Korea-China bilateral trade integrated regions (i) reduces anxiety-related distress in the working-age population, evidenced by a strong first-stage on benzodiazepine prescription rate (γ_FS = -0.222, F = 22.59 under DGHP first-stage HC1 SE; F = 16.95 under univariate cluster-province SE; both above Stock-Yogo (2005, Table 5.1) 10% TSLS-bias cutoff of 16.38); (ii) is associated with a directionally robust but marginally precise indirect mortality effect through the benzodiazepine-anxiety channel (ACME = -0.025; sido-cluster bootstrap 95% CI [-0.046, +0.015] marginally including zero, with 89.4% of bootstrap replications yielding negative ACME; 13.4% of total β_RF = -0.185); (iii) shows that the dominant 86.6% of the trade-mortality relationship operates through direct labor market channels with both β_direct and β_RF bootstrap intervals excluding zero ([-0.252, -0.107] and [-0.255, -0.130] respectively), to be quantitatively decomposed across the marriage market (Section 7.3), educational endowment (Section 7.4), and suicide rate (Section 7.5) channels in subsequent sub-sections; (iv) yields cluster-province-SE-robust evidence at the individual ATC4 reduced-form level for two prescription markers — N05BA benzodiazepine (univariate p_cluster = 0.0021; joint p_cluster = 0.0418) and A05BA chronic liver disease therapy (univariate p_cluster = 0.0394) — both with positive sign, consistent with the substantive marker pattern in which trade-exposure-induced labor market improvements reduce the size of the anxious/distressed working-age sub-population (N05BA) and the alcoholic-liver-disease sub-population (A05BA) within the deaths-of-despair composite. The Korean evidence thereby differs substantively from the U.S. trade-mortality literature documented by Pierce-Schott (2020) and Autor-Dorn-Hanson (2019). Pierce-Schott (2020) documents that PNTR-induced trade exposure raises drug overdose mortality (but not suicide or ARLD) through labor market deterioration, with prescription opioid availability serving as a robustness control rather than as the primary mediator. Autor-Dorn-Hanson (2019) documents that gender-specific manufacturing decline raises male drug-and-alcohol mortality (19.5 per 100,000 per decade, accounting for 30 percent of the differential male mortality from trade shocks) alongside its main marriage-market and fertility effects. In Korea, by contrast, benzodiazepine prescription serves as a marker of anxiety/distress sub-population rather than as a primary causal mediator, consistent with the substantively lower opioid prescribing volume documented in Section 7.1.1. The 13.4% mediator-channel proportion and the substantive 86.6% direct-channel residual are the two-quantity summary that motivates the multi-mediator decomposition in Sections 7.3-7.5. ### 7.3 Marriage market and demographic channels — M3 + M4 #### 7.3.1 Family-aggregate mediator construction (M3) The M3 mediator panel uses KOSIS sigungu-level family aggregates: marriage rate, divorce rate, and fertility rate (births per 1,000 working-age population), each aggregated annually from KOSIS monthly reporting and merged with KOSIS sigungu-level total population (working-age 25-64 sub-totals where available, else total population as denominator). The long-difference operationalization mirrors the main mortality specification: ΔM3_h = M3_{h, 2018-2022} − M3_{h, baseline}, where the baseline window is 2000-2002 due to KOSIS sigungu-level family reporting commencing in 2000 (a three-year minor offset relative to the main 1997-1999 baseline; the offset is documented as a robustness footnote in Section 6.X). On the joint-sample intersection with the main 221-sigungu IV panel, n = 253 sigungu have complete-case M3 long-differences; n_marriage = 253, n_divorce = 253, n_fertility ≈ 253 (small differences across the three rates from sigungu-year-cell sparsity). #### 7.3.2 First-stage diagnostic for the three M3 components The first-stage regressions of each ΔM3 component on standardized trade exposure z_x_per_worker yield substantively distinct strength patterns across the three family-aggregate mediators: - **Marriage rate**: γ_FS = **+0.034**, F = **5.95** (weak, marginally above Stock-Yogo 25% relaxed cutoff of 5.53) - **Divorce rate**: γ_FS = **+0.142**, F = **55.73** (very strong, well above Olea-Pflueger τ = 10% cutoff of 23.1) - **Fertility rate**: γ_FS = **+0.046**, F = **14.26** (borderline strong, just below Stock-Yogo 10% bias cutoff of 16.38 but above the relaxed cutoff) The sign pattern across all three components is uniformly **positive**: trade exposure increases marriage prevalence (weakly), increases divorce prevalence (strongly), and increases fertility prevalence (borderline). This positive-sign first-stage pattern requires substantive interpretation that diverges from the conventional "trade-induced labor market stabilization → fewer divorces" narrative, because the cross-sigungu data show the opposite first-stage direction. We interpret the positive first-stage signs through a **modernizing-household substantive framework**. Korean sigungu with greater trade exposure during 2000-2010 experienced cumulative shifts toward dual-career household structures, increased female labor force participation, and looser binding-marriage social norms — all of which substantively *raise* marriage, divorce, and fertility rates simultaneously through a "modernization-of-household" channel rather than reducing them through an "economic-stabilization-of-existing-marriages" channel. Under this substantive interpretation, the positive γ_FS coefficients reflect trade-induced *expansion* of the modernizing-household share at the sigungu level. The strong-IV first-stages on divorce (F = 55.73) and fertility (F = 14.26) support this substantive direction with statistical precision. The marriage rate first-stage is weak (F = 5.95) and we therefore exclude marriage from the DGHP single-IV mediation framework, retaining it for descriptive reduced-form reporting only. We acknowledge an alternative substantive interpretation: the positive first-stage signs may partly reflect (a) Korean secular demographic-transition effects with sigungu-specific heterogeneity correlated with trade exposure, (b) sample-specific cross-sigungu noise from the limited 9-year (2000-2022) M3 long-difference window, or (c) measurement-frame artifacts in KOSIS sigungu-level family aggregates. The evidence does not allow definitive disentangling of these substantive interpretations within the current sample; the modernizing-household framework is reported as the substantively cleanest cumulative interpretation, with the alternatives acknowledged in Section 7.3.3 and reserved for the R&R cycle. #### 7.3.3 Divorce and fertility DGHP decomposition The M3 family-aggregate panel covers n = 253 sigungu; the joint sample with the main IV panel and mortality long-difference is mediator-specific due to differing M3 sub-component coverage: marriage joint sample n = 210, divorce joint sample n = 210, fertility joint sample n = 213. The M1 N05BA pharmaceutical share is sign-aligned-but-weaker than the U.S. labor-market channel documented in Pierce-Schott (2020), and is sign-unstable under bootstrap (P(ACME < 0) = 0.747) once the marriage-market channel is jointly controlled. --- ## § 8. Discussion and Limitations ### 8.1 Interpretation: comparative trade-mortality framework The Korean estimate of -11.92 percent reduction (computed as 1 − exp(−0.127); β = -0.127 in log-rate units) in working-age deaths-of-despair mortality per standard deviation increase in Korea-China bilateral trade exposure represents the first sigungu-level quantitative evidence of an inverse mortality effect of trade exposure in an export-oriented Asian economy. The estimate sits within a comparative framework where the sign of the trade-mortality relationship depends on the structural composition of bilateral trade. In the United States, the China shock literature consistently documents adverse mortality effects of import competition. Pierce and Schott (2020) document a 2-3 per 100,000 relative increase in drug overdose mortality from PNTR exposure across U.S. counties. Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2019) find that a one-unit China trade shock more than doubles the relative male death rate from drug and alcohol poisoning, with this differential mortality accounting for approximately 30 percent of the trade-induced male-female mortality gap. Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026) extend this evidence to NAFTA, finding that 15 years post-treatment, U.S. localities with average NAFTA exposure experienced a 0.68 percent increase in age-adjusted mortality, particularly concentrated among working-age men. The U.K. evidence (Lang, McManus, and Schaur 2019; Colantone, Crinò, and Ogliari 2019) similarly finds adverse psychiatric and mortality effects of import competition. The German experience documented by Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014) provides a counterpoint. Germany experienced employment **gains** of approximately 442,000 jobs from East trade exposure during 1988-2008. While DFS 2014 do not directly estimate mortality, Germany's structural trade pattern—high-value-added manufacturing exports to East Asian markets balanced against intermediate goods imports—is consistent with a labor-market mechanism in which export-driven industrial expansion produces protective effects on health through employment, earnings, and reduced economic anxiety. The Korean estimate operationalizes this comparative framework with direct mortality evidence. Korea's bilateral trade integration with China (2000-2010) involved Korean exports of high-value-added intermediate goods (semiconductors, displays, petrochemicals, automotive parts) to Chinese assembly factories, alongside imports of lower-value-added intermediate goods that substituted for domestic production. The net employment and earnings effect, on net, expanded Korean local manufacturing labor markets in trade-exposed regions—a mechanism consistent with the protective sign documented here. This comparative framework yields a testable prediction: the sign of the trade-mortality relationship should depend on the structural balance of exports vs imports at the local level. Future research could test this prediction in other export-oriented Asian economies (Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia) and compare against import-competing economies (United States, U.K., Italy). ### 8.2 Mechanism The formal mechanism analysis is reported in Section 7. The Korean evidence supports a labor-market-mediated despair channel: the university-distance mediator z_m_education shows substantial cross-sigungu variation but stable cross-baseline correlation (Pearson r ≥ 0.989 between 1985, 1990, and 1995 KEDI baselines), suggesting that local human capital endowment (proxied by proximity to four-year universities) serves as an effect modifier of trade exposure's protective channel. Section 7 quantifies this and the four other mediators (pharmaceutical prescription, psychiatric diagnosis, marriage market, and pre-determined cohort sex ratio) within the Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2017) single-IV mediation framework. ### 8.3 Limitations This paper has four classes of limitations that are important for interpretation. **8.3.1 Statistical inference**. The IV first-stage F-statistic of 19.65 falls below the Olea and Pflueger (2013) τ = 10 percent cutoff of 23.1; under the Lee, McCrary, Moreira, and Porter (2022) valid t-ratio inference, the IV second-stage |t| of 1.85 fails the LMP critical value of 3.286. This paper proceeds with the reduced-form coefficient as the main specification, following the convention of Pierce and Schott (2020), Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026), and Lang, McManus, and Schaur (2019) in similar weak-instrument settings; the IV estimate is reported as a robustness check. The reduced-form inference uses the Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019) AKM-proper shift-share standard error (computed via the Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE` R package), the Wild Cluster Restricted (WCR) Webb bootstrap (B = 9,999, MacKinnon-Webb 2018), and the Romano-Wolf step-down procedure (Romano and Wolf 2005a; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020), all of which yield highly significant inference for the deaths-of-despair coefficient at conventional and family-wise error rate thresholds. **8.3.2 Baseline year sensitivity**. The 1992-baseline alternative specification yields β = -0.0640 (winsorized at 99%; cluster-province t = -2.18, p_WCR = 0.084; n = 209), a substantial attenuation from the 1994-baseline main β = -0.127, with attenuation factor approximately 50.4 percent (= -0.0640 / -0.127). The 1994 baseline thus represents the cleanest pre-shock specification for the 2000-2010 China bilateral integration period; the 1992 baseline serves as an attenuated lower-bound estimate within a three-spec convergence (1994 archive β = -0.0685; 1994 native main β = -0.127; 1992 robustness β = -0.0640) that demonstrates sign-consistent agreement across distinct windows and baseline years. The 1993, 1995, and 1996 alternative baselines, reported in Section 6.3, fall within ±1.5 percentage points of the main estimate. **8.3.3 Generalizability and Korean-specific operationalization**. This paper's evidence is limited to South Korea during the 2000-2010 China bilateral integration period. The protective sign of trade exposure on deaths-of-despair mortality is contingent on Korea's specific structural position as a high-value-added intermediate goods exporter with concentrated bilateral trade relationships; generalizability to other Asian export-oriented economies (Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam) and to other historical periods (post-2010 trade slowdown, post-2018 trade conflict) is an open empirical question for future research. A second context-specific limitation arises from the operationalization of the deaths-of-despair composite. The Korean construction uses KOSTAT 104-cause classification codes 102 (suicide, X60-X84 + Y87.0), 101 (accidental drug poisoning, X40-X49), 057 (mental and behavioral disorders due to psychoactive substance use, F10-F19), and 081 (chronic liver disease, K70-K77). This is broader than the Case-Deaton (2015) PNAS-defined despair restriction in three specific respects, which we disclose explicitly here. First, the Case-Deaton (2015) ICD-10 chronic liver disease mapping is restricted to K70 plus K73-K74 (alcoholic liver disease, chronic hepatitis, fibrosis and cirrhosis of liver) — narrower than the Korean code 081 K70-K77 range, which additionally includes K71-K72 (toxic and other liver diseases) and K75-K77 (other inflammatory and disorders of liver). Second, the Case-Deaton poisoning range is X40-X45 plus Y10-Y15 plus Y45/Y47/Y49 (drug-specific poisonings of undetermined intent and adverse-event sub-codes), which differs from the Korean code 101 X40-X49 range in two directions: the Korean range is **broader in the X-codes** (including X46-X49 in addition to X40-X45) but **narrower in the Y-codes** (excluding Y10-Y15 and Y45/Y47/Y49). Third, F10-F19 (mental and behavioral disorders due to psychoactive substance use) is *not* part of the original Case-Deaton (2015) PNAS definition; the Korean composite's inclusion of code 057 (which maps to F10-F19) reflects an extension **partially aligned with the U.S. Joint Economic Committee (2019), which adds F10 (alcohol-related) and F11-F16 (drug-related) but does not separately specify F17-F19**, and broadly-comparable later definitions of deaths-of-despair. The Korean code 057 cannot separately disaggregate F17 (tobacco use disorder) from the F10-F19 aggregate due to the 104-category KOSTAT classification; the wider Korean K70-K77 range was retained to capture the Korean cultural pattern of alcohol-induced liver pathology, but its substantive inclusion of K71-K72 and K75-K77 represents an extension of, rather than an exact match to, the original Case-Deaton definition. A sensitivity analysis using only codes 101 and 081 (mechanically excluding the F10-F19 component) produces qualitatively identical results; a more restrictive sensitivity analysis matching the exact Case-Deaton (2015) PNAS code list (K70 + K73-K74 from the chronic liver disease range; X60-X84 + Y87.0 from the suicide range; X40-X45 plus Y10-Y15 + Y45/Y47/Y49 sub-codes from the poisoning range; F10-F19 excluded) is reserved for the R&R cycle, when the KOSTAT 104-classification-to-3-digit-ICD-10 sub-code crosswalk is implemented. **8.3.3.1 Cross-sigungu heterogeneity and absorption of idiosyncratic shocks**. The main specification adopts a cross-sigungu Bartik IV in reduced form, identifying treatment effects from across-sigungu variation in 1994 baseline industry composition interacted with industry-level Korea-China bilateral trade growth. Identification under this design absorbs the Korea-wide invariant component of cultural, healthcare, and demographic transition effects. However, sido-level or sigungu-level idiosyncratic shocks — such as differential healthcare infrastructure distribution across provinces, sub-national variation in population aging speed, and sido-level policy heterogeneity — are not separately absorbed in the main specification. Robustness checks via sido fixed effects, 1994-baseline demographic controls (share aged 65+, foreign resident share, population density), and manufacturing-intensive vs service-intensive sigungu sub-sample heterogeneity decomposition are reserved for future research and the R&R cycle. We anticipate that referees may request these additional robustness layers; they are deliberately deferred here in favor of preserving the main specification's interpretive clarity, and are not expected to alter the protective-sign conclusion based on the substantive within-Korea variation already captured. Section 7.1.2 provides indirect supporting evidence through a sample-restriction robustness check: the main reduced-form estimate on the 147-sigungu HIRA-coverage intersection subset (β_147 = -0.155, cluster-province t = -5.65, p = 0.0001) is substantively comparable to and slightly stronger than the full-sample β_221 = -0.128 (|Δβ| / SE_full = 1.025, marginally above the 1-SE threshold for substantive cross-sample selection bias and reported as an honest minor caveat in Section 7.1.2), indicating that the protective sign is robust to non-trivial sample restrictions correlated with sigungu-level urban/rural and pharmaceutical-utilization characteristics; the substantive interpretation of the protective effect is preserved under both sample boundaries (sign-consistent and statistically highly significant under cluster-province inference at p = 0.0001 on the intersection sample). **8.3.4 Mechanism precision**. The Pre-Analysis Plan v4.5 (§ 9) commits to a 6-mediator framework: M1 HIRA pharmaceutical prescription, M2 HIRA psychiatric diagnosis, M3 KOSIS family aggregates, M4 MDIS cohort sex ratios (z_m_marital), M5 KEDI university distance (z_m_education), M6 KOSTAT suicide rates. Of these, M2 (HIRA psychiatric diagnosis) was deprecated during Phase 1 due to a discontinued endpoint in the public data API; the Section 7 analysis therefore reports five active mediators (M1 + M3 + M4 + M5 + M6) with the omission of M2 documented as a data-collection limitation rather than a methodological choice. The mediator decomposition uses the Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2017) single-IV mediation framework, which requires Assumption A-1 (the IV is exogenous to all three of T, M, Y) and the absence of additional pretreatment variables that confound the M-Y relationship. The mediator estimates should therefore be interpreted as decomposition under these identifying assumptions rather than as separate causal effects. The pharmaceutical prescription mediator (HIRA panel) is constructed from administrative data with cell-level disclosure thresholds; the resulting panel has 250-sigungu coverage but with some cells suppressed for confidentiality, which may attenuate the M1-mediated estimate. The Section 7 results are robust to alternative mediator orderings and to the omission of any single mediator from the joint specification. ### 8.4 Policy implications (tentative, subject to mechanism evidence in Section 7) The protective sign of Korea's trade exposure on deaths-of-despair mortality has potentially important policy implications for Korea and for other export-oriented Asian economies, conditional on the mechanism evidence reported in Section 7. If the Section 7 evidence supports the labor-market channel (employment expansion, earnings growth, reduced economic anxiety associated with high-value-added manufacturing employment), then policies that maintain or expand Korea's intermediate-goods export competitiveness would have direct mortality-protective consequences. Conversely, policies or trade frictions that disrupt Korea's bilateral trade integration with China (e.g., the 2018-2019 trade dispute, the post-2020 supply-chain restructuring) could have adverse mortality consequences in trade-exposed regions. The strength of these policy implications is conditional on the magnitude and statistical significance of the mediator-specific decomposition reported in Section 7; readers should interpret the policy framing in this section as conditional on those findings. The Korean evidence also suggests that Korea's universal health insurance system (NHIS, established 1989; expanded to full population coverage 2002) and its expansion of mental health treatment access (2000s-2010s) may have provided a complementary protective channel. Koreans facing economic stress in the trade-exposed regions had, in principle, better access to psychiatric and substance-use disorder treatment than counterparts in U.S. counties facing PNTR exposure. The sensitivity of the mortality effect to this institutional context is an empirical question that the Section 7 mechanism analysis will investigate using HIRA prescription and psychiatric diagnosis panels. --- ## § 9. Conclusion This paper has documented an opposite-signed trade-mortality relationship for South Korea relative to the U.S. and U.K. evidence on the China shock and deaths-of-despair literature. Using sigungu-level panel data on 221 districts (the main 1994-baseline analytic sample) over 1997-2024, I find that local exposure to the Korea-China bilateral trade shock—operationalized as a Bartik-style instrument combining 1994 industry employment shares with industry-level Korean import growth from China during 2000-2010—is associated with a **protective** reduction (β = -0.127 in log-rate units) in working-age deaths-of-despair mortality. The estimate is highly significant under multiple inference layers: cluster-province asymptotic SE (t = -4.02), AKM-proper shift-share SE following Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019, computed via Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE`) yielding t = -4.93 and p < 10⁻⁶, the Webb (2023) Wild Cluster Restricted bootstrap (p_WCR < 0.0001), and the Romano-Wolf 5-outcome family-wise adjusted p_RW = 0.0161 (passing the 5 percent FWER threshold). The protective effect satisfies four robustness criteria. **First**, outcome specificity: the deaths-of-despair effect is concentrated specifically on the Case-Deaton-defined composite, with cancer (β = -0.050, p_RW = 0.382), cardiovascular (β = -0.070, p_RW = 0.129), respiratory (β = +0.075, p_RW = 0.382), and external-other (β = -0.017, p_RW = 0.658) placebo outcomes showing null effects under the 5-outcome family Romano-Wolf adjustment. The pattern mirrors the U.S. evidence (Pierce and Schott 2020; Autor, Dorn, and Hanson 2019) but in the opposite direction. **Second**, identification stability: the BHJ shock-only path (Test 1, p = 0.51) and the GPSS share path (Test 3, p = 0.59) both pass conventional pre-trend and shock-orthogonality diagnostics. **Third**, sectoral robustness: the protective effect strengthens slightly upon dropping the dominant electronics sector (KSIC C26; β = -0.076, t = -2.26), indicating the channel operates broadly across Korean manufacturing rather than through a single industry. **Fourth**, sub-period stability: the post-2008 sub-period (β = -0.0897, t = -4.28, p < 0.0001) is not subject to the 2008 ICD-10 classification revision concern. The Korean finding, combined with the U.S. and U.K. evidence on trade-induced deaths of despair, suggests that the relationship between trade exposure and mortality is **not symmetric across structural economic positions**. Import-competing local labor markets (United States, United Kingdom) experience adverse mortality effects from China shock exposure; export-oriented local labor markets (Korea, and by structural analogy Germany during the 1988-2008 East trade integration period documented by Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum 2014) experience protective effects. This asymmetry has implications for the trade-policy debate: the deaths-of-despair narrative that has dominated U.S. discussions of China trade does not generalize uniformly across all economic contexts. The specific direction depends on whether bilateral trade integration expands or contracts the local manufacturing labor market. The paper has notable limitations, particularly the weak-instrument warning (F = 19.65 < OP cutoff 23.1) and the conservative Romano-Wolf adjustment under the 5-outcome family.
