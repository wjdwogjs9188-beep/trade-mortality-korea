Dear Editor,

I am pleased to submit the enclosed manuscript for consideration in the *Korean Economic Review*. This paper provides the first sigungu-level quantitative evidence on the trade-mortality relationship in South Korea over the 1997–2022 period, documenting a protective effect of Korea–China bilateral trade exposure on working-age deaths-of-despair mortality that runs *opposite* to the well-established adverse U.S. and U.K. evidence (Pierce and Schott 2020; Autor, Dorn, and Hanson 2019; Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi 2026; Lang, McManus, and Schaur 2019). The manuscript directly addresses a substantive gap in the China-shock and deaths-of-despair literature: whether the adverse trade-mortality nexus generalizes uniformly across structural economic positions, or whether export-oriented economies experience a structurally distinct trajectory.

**Substantive contributions.** The paper makes four contributions. *First*, using a sigungu-level Bartik instrument that combines 1994 baseline industry employment shares with industry-level Korean import growth from China during 2000–2010, the paper documents a long-difference protective coefficient of β = −0.127 on working-age deaths-of-despair mortality (cluster-province t = −4.02; AKM-proper shift-share SE following Adão, Kolesár, and Morales 2019, t = −4.93, p < 10⁻⁶; Webb 2023 Wild Cluster Restricted bootstrap p < 0.0001), which passes the 5-percent family-wise error rate threshold under Romano–Wolf step-down adjustment over a five-outcome family (p_RW = 0.0161). *Second*, the paper articulates a comparative trade-mortality framework in which the sign of the relationship depends on whether bilateral trade integration expands or contracts the local manufacturing labor market, paralleling Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014) on German export gains while diverging from the U.S. import-competing evidence. *Third*, the inference workflow combines AKM-proper shift-share standard errors for single-coefficient inference with Romano–Wolf step-down under a Webb (2023) 6-point Wild Cluster Restricted bootstrap backend for family-wise inference — to our knowledge, this combination has not been adopted as a standard reference workflow in the trade-mortality or shift-share Bartik-IV literature. *Fourth*, the mechanism analysis decomposes the protective channel through a Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2017) single-IV mediation framework over five mediator panels (HIRA pharmaceutical prescription, KOSIS family aggregates, MDIS pre-determined cohort sex ratios, KEDI university-distance endowment, and KOSTAT suicide rates), with a joint multivariate specification documenting that approximately 59 percent of the protective effect operates through identified mediators (dominated by the marriage-market pathway at 38.4 percent) and 42 percent through residual direct labor-market and broader social channels.

**Fit with KER scope.** The paper's substantive domain — Korean local labor markets, Korean public-health mortality outcomes, Korean trade integration with China — places it directly within KER's editorial mandate on Korean economic research. The methodological contribution and the comparative cross-country framework should also be of interest to readers working on shift-share Bartik-IV identification, deaths of despair, and the broader trade-and-mortality literature beyond the Korean context.

**Pre-registration and transparency.** The pre-analysis plan (PAP v4.5, registered on OSF on May 5, 2026) commits the primary outcome (deaths-of-despair composite), the four secondary placebo outcomes, the inference framework, the 1994 baseline year choice, and the six-mediator framework prior to the present analysis. All Stata, R, and Python code, together with the master analytic panel and intermediate datasets, will be made available through a public replication repository upon acceptance, subject to KOSTAT and HIRA microdata usage terms.

**Declarations.** This manuscript is the original work of the sole author, has not been published elsewhere, and is not under simultaneous review at any other journal. The author declares no competing interests. The research uses publicly accessible aggregate data from KOSIS, KOSTAT, HIRA, KEDI, MDIS, and UN Comtrade, and the institutional review of the Gachon University Department of Economics has confirmed that no human-subjects ethical review is required for this aggregate-level analysis. No external funding supported this research.

I hope the manuscript meets the standards of the *Korean Economic Review*, and I would be grateful for the editor's and referees' consideration. I am happy to provide any additional materials or clarifications upon request.
