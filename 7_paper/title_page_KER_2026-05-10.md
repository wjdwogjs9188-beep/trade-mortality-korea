### Abstract

This paper provides the first causal evidence of the "deaths of despair" phenomenon in South Korea, leveraging Korea-China bilateral trade integration (1992-2022, Bartik shock window 2000-2010) as a natural experiment. Using a highly granular sigungu-level Shift-Share (Bartik) instrumental variable design on 221 districts, I document a robust *protective* effect of trade-induced labor market expansion on working-age mortality (β = -0.127, AKM-proper t = -4.93, p < 10⁻⁶), structurally diverging from the adverse outcomes observed in the United States and the United Kingdom. I apply a 5-outcome family Romano-Wolf adjustment (p_RW = 0.0161) to confirm that this protective effect is highly specific to the Case-Deaton mortality composite (suicide, alcohol-related liver disease, and drug poisonings), with placebo outcomes exhibiting strict null effects. Furthermore, utilizing a Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2017) joint multi-mediator framework, I identify a uniquely "Korean" mechanism. While U.S. mortality is predominantly driven by labor market deterioration with prescription opioid availability as a robustness control (Pierce-Schott 2020) and by marriage market and fertility contraction (Autor-Dorn-Hanson 2019), the Korean trade-mortality nexus is overwhelmingly mediated by family formation and marriage market modernization channels, which account for approximately 59% of the identified effect. These findings provide novel cross-country evidence on the structural determinants of mortality in an export-driven economy, highlighting the primacy of demographic resilience over pharmaceutical channels in buffering labor market transitions.
