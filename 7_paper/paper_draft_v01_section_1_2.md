## § 1. Introduction

The literature on trade exposure and worker outcomes documents three robust findings. First, manufacturing decline from import competition reduces local employment and earnings (Autor, Dorn, and Hanson 2013, Pierce and Schott 2016, Charles, Hurst, and Schwartz 2019). Second, this decline causally elevates "deaths of despair"—suicide, drug overdose, and alcohol-related liver disease—in the affected regions. The U.S. evidence is particularly strong: Pierce and Schott (2020) document that an interquartile shift in U.S. counties' exposure to permanent normal trade relations (PNTR) with China is associated with a 2-3 per 100,000 relative increase in mortality from drug overdoses, with little measurable effect on suicide or alcohol-related liver disease. Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2019) find that a one-unit China trade shock more than doubles the relative male death rate from drug and alcohol poisoning in U.S. commuting zones, and that this differential mortality accounts for roughly 30 percent of the trade-induced male-female mortality gap. Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026) extend this to NAFTA, finding that 15 years post-treatment, U.S. localities with average NAFTA exposure experienced a 0.68 percent increase in age-adjusted mortality, with the effects particularly concentrated among working-age men. Third, mediating channels include workplace stress and mental distress (Lang, McManus, and Schaur 2019; Colantone, Crinò, and Ogliari 2019), occupational injury (McManus and Schaur 2016), and individual-level displacement (Sullivan and von Wachter 2009; Eliason and Storrie 2009).

This paper documents an opposite finding for South Korea, an export-oriented economy with bilateral trade integration with China. Using Korean census-based panel data on 221 districts (sigungu) over 1997-2024, I find that local exposure to the Korea-China bilateral trade shock—operationalized as a Bartik-style instrument combining 1994 industry employment shares with industry-level Korean import growth from China during 2000-2010—is associated with a *protective* effect on deaths of despair. A one standard deviation increase in trade exposure reduces the long-difference change in log working-age (25-64) deaths-of-despair mortality by 0.127 (cluster-province t = -4.02 under cluster-robust inference at the G = 16 province level). The estimate is highly significant under multiple inference layers: the Adão-Kolesár-Morales (2019) shift-share standard error (computed using the Kolesár 2024 `ShiftShareSE` R package) yields SE = 0.0258, t = -4.93, p < 10⁻⁶. The Webb (2023) 6-point Wild Cluster Restricted bootstrap at the province level (B = 9,999 replications, MacKinnon-Webb 2018 implementation) yields p_WCR < 0.0001 (zero of 9,999 bootstrap iterations exceed the observed |t|). Under the 5-outcome family Romano-Wolf step-down adjustment with WCR bootstrap (Romano-Wolf 2005a Algorithm 4.1; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020), the deaths-of-despair coefficient remains significant at the 5 percent family-wise error rate (p_RW = 0.0161). The effect is broad-based: it survives dropping the dominant electronics sector (KSIC C26) and A pre-WTO placebo specification using 1992-1996 China bilateral imports as a falsification instrument yields a placebo coefficient of -0.123 (cluster-province t = -3.50, p_WCR = 0.0004), with sign aligned to the main estimate; we report this honestly as a placebo failure under the strict pre-WTO activation-timing interpretation and discuss the alternative gradual-integration interpretation in Section 6.1.

The Korean finding contrasts sharply with the U.S. and U.K. evidence but is consistent in mechanism with German evidence. Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014) document that Germany—an export-oriented economy with strong East Asian trade integration—experienced employment *gains* of approximately 442,000 jobs from East trade exposure during 1988-2008. Although DFS 2014 do not directly measure mortality, the Korean evidence is consistent with a mechanism in which export-driven labor market expansion produces protective effects on health that mirror the adverse effects observed in import-shocked U.S. and U.K. economies. Korea's bilateral integration with China—through which Korea exports intermediate goods (semiconductors, displays, automotive parts, petrochemicals) that are assembled in Chinese factories and re-exported globally—differs structurally from the U.S. import competition that Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013) document.

This paper contributes to three literatures.

First, the paper provides the first sigungu-level quantitative evidence on the inverse mortality effect of trade exposure in an export-oriented economy. While Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014) document the employment side of the German experience and Sufi (2023) examines Korea's household debt response to trade shocks, neither paper directly estimates mortality outcomes at the sub-national level. The Korean estimate of -11.9 percent mortality decline per standard deviation trade exposure (computed as 1 − exp(−0.127); exact value −11.92 percent; equivalently, an interquartile-range shift in bilateral exposure between 25th-percentile and 75th-percentile sigungu corresponds to approximately −8.78 percent mortality difference under the ADH 2013 / Pierce-Schott 2020 IQR-translation convention; native-unit interpretation in Section 5.1 Footnote X) is a quantitative complement to the U.S. estimates of +5-9 percent (Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi 2026; ADH 2019; Pierce and Schott 2020) and is consistent with what Korean policymakers and demographers have anecdotally observed but never formally measured. Combined with the U.S. evidence, the Korean finding suggests that the relationship between trade exposure and deaths of despair is not symmetric across economic structures: import-competing local labor markets experience adverse effects, while export-oriented local labor markets experience protective effects.

Second, the paper makes a methodological contribution by combining the recently developed valid t-ratio inference for instrumental variables (Lee, McCrary, Moreira, and Porter 2022) with a direct pre-period placebo test of the Borusyak, Hull, and Jaravel (2022) shock-only exogeneity assumption. The Korean Bartik IV first-stage F-statistic of 19.65 falls below the Olea and Pflueger (2013) τ = 10 percent cutoff of 23.1, placing the IV in weak-instrument territory. Following Lee et al. (2022) Table 3 Panel A, the corresponding 5 percent critical value is c₀.₀₅(F) = 3.286. The IV second-stage |t| of 1.85 fails this LMP threshold, consistent with weak-instrument inference. However, this paper proceeds with reduced-form inference as the main specification—a convention adopted by Pierce and Schott (2020) and Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026) in similar settings—and reports IV estimates as robustness. The Korean F-statistic is comparable to Lang, McManus, and Schaur (2019), who report a first-stage F of 18.77 in their fully controlled specification (Table 2, column M.3) using the identical 8-OECD ADH-style instrument list (Australia, Switzerland, Germany, Denmark, Spain, Finland, Japan, New Zealand). The procedural consistency between this paper and Lang et al. (2019) provides a published precedent for the weak-instrument inference protocol applied here.

Third, the paper documents outcome specificity that mirrors the Case-Deaton (2015) deaths-of-despair fingerprint observed in the United States but in the inverse direction. While the U.S. literature finds adverse effects of trade exposure concentrated specifically on suicide, drug-related deaths, and alcohol-related liver disease—with cancer, cardiovascular, and respiratory deaths showing little response—the Korean evidence in this paper finds protective effects concentrated specifically on the deaths-of-despair composite. Cancer, cardiovascular, respiratory, and external-other (non-suicide) mortality categories show no statistically significant response to the trade exposure shock under Romano-Wolf step-down adjustments for multiple-hypothesis testing across the five-outcome family (Romano and Wolf 2005a, 2005b, 2016; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020). This outcome specificity strengthens the interpretation that the labor market channel—rather than general mortality conditions—drives the result.

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the Korean trade context and motivates the export-driven economy framing. Section 3 describes the data sources, including KOSTAT mortality microdata, KOSIS population panels, the Comtrade-based bilateral trade measures, and the Korean Industrial Productivity (KIP) industry classification. Section 4 develops the identification strategy, including the Bartik IV construction, the LMP 2022 valid t-ratio inference, and the Borusyak-Hull-Jaravel 2022 shock-only diagnostics. Section 5 presents the main empirical results. Section 6 reports robustness checks: dropping the electronics sector, alternative baseline years (1992-1996 sensitivity), pre-WTO placebo specifications, and outcome specificity. Section 7 explores mechanism through six separate channels: pharmaceutical prescription rates (HIRA), psychiatric diagnosis rates (HIRA, province-level), marriage market measures (KOSIS family aggregates), pre-determined cohort sex ratios (z_m_marital from MDIS census), and university-distance baseline characteristics (z_m_education from KEDI 1985 yearbook). The mechanism analysis follows the Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2017) single-IV mediation framework. Section 8 discusses limitations and policy implications. Section 9 concludes.

## § 2. Background and Korean Trade Context

### 2.1 Korea's bilateral integration with China

Following the establishment of diplomatic relations between South Korea and China in 1992 and China's accession to the World Trade Organization in 2001, Korea-China bilateral trade expanded at unprecedented rates. The bilateral trade volume between Korea and China grew dramatically over the 2000-2010 integration period — Korean imports from China expanded from approximately USD 12.8 billion to USD 71.6 billion, while Korean exports to China grew from USD 18.5 billion to USD 116.8 billion (UN Comtrade, KOSTAT). To capture the long-run mortality response to this bilateral integration, this paper adopts a long-difference research design with a 21-year window — using 1997-1999 (3-year average) as the pre-integration baseline and 2018-2022 (5-year average) as the post-integration endpoint period. This window length encompasses the full bilateral trade scale-up of 2000-2010 plus the subsequent decade of long-run cumulative mortality response, and is anchored on the 1994 industrial census for the share-weighted Bartik exposure measure. By 2010, China became Korea's largest trading partner in both directions, surpassing the United States and Japan combined.

The structural composition of this trade differs substantially from the U.S.-China trade pattern that motivates the Autor-Dorn-Hanson (2013) China shock literature. Korean imports from China are dominated by intermediate goods—machinery components, electronic parts, basic chemicals, and steel—that enter Korean manufacturing processes for subsequent re-export. Korean exports to China are dominated by high-value-added intermediate goods (semiconductors, displays, petrochemical derivatives, automotive parts) that are then assembled in Chinese factories for export to Western markets. This bilateral integration means that Korea-China trade volume increases reflect both increased final-goods imports (substituting for domestic production) and increased global value chain participation (creating Korean export demand). The latter effect dominates in net employment terms, particularly during 2000-2010 when Korea's manufacturing sector expanded its share of high-technology exports while Chinese imports replaced lower-value-added segments.

This structural feature distinguishes the Korean experience from the U.S. and U.K. cases studied in the Bartik-IV deaths-of-despair literature. In the U.S. setting documented by Pierce and Schott (2016, 2020), Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013, 2019), Charles, Hurst, and Schwartz (2019), and Finkelstein, Notowidigdo, and Shi (2026), local labor markets faced sharp manufacturing employment declines as Chinese imports replaced domestic production. The Korean situation is closer to that of Germany during the 1988-2008 East trade integration period documented by Dauth, Findeisen, and Suedekum (2014): a structural shift in trading partner composition that, on net, expanded rather than contracted local manufacturing employment in trade-exposed regions.

### 2.2 Manufacturing employment trends in Korea

Korean manufacturing employment grew from approximately 4.0 million workers in 2000 to 4.5 million in 2010, with regional variation reflecting both pre-existing industrial composition and differential exposure to China bilateral trade growth. The Korean Industrial Census (광업제조업조사, conducted annually by KOSTAT) provides establishment-level employment data at the sigungu level for the manufacturing sector (KSIC division C; previously KSIC division D under the 6th classification revision). I use the 1994 KOSTAT industrial census as the baseline year for constructing the Bartik instrument shares, following the convention in the Bartik-IV literature of using a pre-treatment baseline (Borusyak, Hull, and Jaravel 2025; Goldsmith-Pinkham, Sorkin, and Swift 2020). The choice of 1994 reflects a balance between pre-treatment timing (well before the 2000 onset of major China bilateral integration) and data quality (the 1994 census employs the consolidated industrial classification later harmonized into KSIC 9th and 10th revisions).

A potential concern with the 1994 baseline is that it falls within three years of the 1997 Asian financial crisis, which substantially restructured Korean manufacturing. To address this, I conduct sensitivity analysis using alternative baselines from 1992, 1993, 1995, and 1996 (Section 6). The 1992 and 1993 baselines pre-date the IMF crisis entirely and provide cleaner pre-shock industrial composition measures. The 1995 and 1996 baselines fall within the year prior to the IMF crisis and may reflect short-run anticipatory adjustments. Using all five baselines, I find that the main estimate of -6.9 percent is stable across alternative baseline years (Section 6).

### 2.3 Deaths of despair in the Korean context

The composite category of "deaths of despair," coined by Case and Deaton (2015) for the United States, comprises three causes: drug overdose, suicide, and alcohol-related liver disease. The Korean operationalization of this composite must accommodate the Korean classification system that aggregates causes of death into 104 categories. I define `despair_total` as the sum of four KOSTAT cause-of-death codes:

- Code 102 (suicide): X60-X84 in the ICD-10 classification
- Code 101 (accidental drug poisoning): X40-X49
- Code 057 (mental and behavioral disorders due to psychoactive substance use): F10-F19
- Code 081 (chronic liver disease): K70-K77

This Korean composite is broader than Case and Deaton's (2015) PNAS-defined despair category. Case and Deaton restrict the alcohol-related liver disease component to ICD-10 codes K70 (alcoholic liver disease) plus K73 (chronic hepatitis) plus K74 (fibrosis and cirrhosis), excluding K71 (toxic liver disease), K72 (hepatic failure), K75 (inflammatory liver disease), K76 (other liver disease), and K77 (liver disorders in diseases classified elsewhere). The Korean cause code 081 spans the broader K70-K77 range, intentionally adopted in this paper to capture the full Korean cultural pattern of alcohol-induced liver pathology, which differs from the U.S. pattern in part because of distinct alcohol consumption norms and earlier disease detection through Korea's universal health insurance system. A sensitivity analysis using narrower despair definitions (suicide only; drug + alcohol liver only) is presented in Section 6.

The Korean code 057 captures the F10-F19 ICD-10 range (mental and behavioral disorders due to psychoactive substance use), which includes alcohol use disorder (F10), opioid use disorder (F11), cannabis use disorder (F12), and other substance use disorders. This Korean code is broader than the strict U.S. despair definition because it does not separate F17 (tobacco use disorder), which Case and Deaton (2015) explicitly exclude from their despair composite. The Korean microdata system aggregates F10-F19 into a single code, preventing exact replication of the Case-Deaton restriction. Section 8 discusses this limitation and notes its negligible empirical importance: a sensitivity analysis using only codes 101 and 081 (mechanically excluding the F10-F19 mental disorder component) produces qualitatively identical results.

### 2.4 Outcome groups for outcome-specificity testing

To test whether the trade exposure effect is concentrated specifically on the deaths-of-despair composite rather than on general mortality conditions, I construct four placebo outcome categories using the same KOSTAT 104-cause classification:

- `cancer` (codes 027-048; ICD-10 C00-C97): All malignant neoplasms
- `cardiovascular` (codes 067-070; ICD-10 I20-I52): Ischemic heart disease and other cardiovascular pathologies
- `respiratory` (codes 073-078; ICD-10 J00-J99): All respiratory diseases
- `external_other` (codes 097-104 minus 102; ICD-10 V01-Y89 minus X60-X84): External causes of mortality excluding suicide

If trade exposure effects on Korean mortality reflect general mortality conditions—rising income, declining environmental pollution, improving health care access—the four placebo outcomes should also respond to trade exposure, even if more weakly. Conversely, if the effect operates specifically through the labor-market channel that Case-Deaton (2015) identify in the U.S. context, the four placebo outcomes should show no statistically significant response after multiple-hypothesis adjustment for the five-outcome family.

The Romano-Wolf step-down procedure (Romano and Wolf 2005a, 2005b, 2016; Clarke, Romano, and Wolf 2020) provides family-wise error rate (FWER) control at the 5 percent level. I implement Algorithm 4.1-4.2 of Romano and Wolf (2005b), which applies studentized step-down testing to the five-outcome family using 1,000 cluster-province wild bootstrap iterations. The resulting adjusted p-values control the FWER at 5 percent across all five outcomes simultaneously. Section 5 reports both unadjusted and Romano-Wolf adjusted inference; Section 6 presents the full robustness pattern.

### 2.5 The 2008 ICD-10 revision

A potential concern in the panel mortality data is the 4th-to-5th revision of the Korean Standard Classification of Diseases (KCD), implemented by KOSTAT in 2008. The revision affected approximately 12 percent of cause-of-death codes in the deaths-of-despair categories, primarily through more precise sub-classification of substance use disorders (code 057). To assess whether the main estimate reflects a mechanical artifact of this classification break, I conduct a sub-period split analysis: estimating the main specification separately for the 1997-2007 (pre-revision) and 2008-2018 (post-revision) sub-periods. The sub-period estimates show consistent negative signs and similar magnitudes, confirming that the main result does not arise from the 2008 classification break (Section 6).
